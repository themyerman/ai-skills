# Content writing: blog posts, social media, and web copy

## 1. Blog posts

**Lead with the thing, not the preamble.** "In this post I will explain…" is throat-clearing — cut it. Open with the hook (a fact, a question, a moment), then the context, then what the reader gets.

**One idea per post.** If you find yourself writing "and also…" for the third time, you have two posts. Publish the smaller one first.

**Structure that works:**
1. Hook — the specific detail that earns attention
2. Context — why this matters or where it comes from
3. The substance — what you learned, made, or decided
4. One close — a takeaway, a question, or the next thing; not a summary of what you just said

**Concrete beats abstract.** "I painted this over six weeks while my kids were asleep" lands harder than "it took a long time." Specific details are what make a post memorable.

**Conversational but not sloppy.** Contractions are fine. Slang dates fast — skip it unless it's genuinely yours. Read the draft aloud; if you'd never say it that way, rewrite it.

**Length:** as long as the idea needs, no longer. Most blog posts could lose 20% and be better for it.

## 2. Social media

### General

- **Lead with the thing.** The first sentence is what gets read — on every platform. Don't bury the image description, the news, or the question in line three.
- **Keep your voice consistent across platforms.** You shouldn't sound like a different person on Instagram vs. Mastodon. Adjust format and length; don't adjust personality.
- **Don't verbatim cross-post.** Twitter-style threads, LinkedIn corporate speak, and Instagram caption conventions don't translate. Rewrite for the platform, even if the idea is the same.

### Mastodon / federated platforms

- First sentence is the hook — it's what shows in previews and boosts.
- Use **content warnings (CW)** for anything that could surprise someone: politics, mental health, eye contact in images, food, spoilers. CW text should describe, not tease ("food photo" not "oh no").
- **Alt text on every image** — the Mastodon community takes this seriously, and it's the right thing to do.
- Hashtags work here; keep them to 3–5 and put them at the end.
- Threads work but aren't native — a single well-written post usually beats a thread.

### Instagram

- **First line is above the fold** — it's what shows before "more." Make it worth tapping.
- Captions can be longer than most people use. Story and context perform well for artists and creators.
- **Hashtags at the end** of the caption or in the first comment — not scattered through the text.
- The image does the primary work; the caption adds depth, story, or context. Don't describe what's already visible.
- Line breaks matter — a wall of text gets skipped. Short paragraphs, breathing room.

### Patreon posts

- **Patrons already believe in you** — skip the sell. Give them something real: process, story, struggle, the thing behind the thing.
- **Exclusivity has value.** If they can read it anywhere, why pay? Early access, unfinished work, decisions in progress — that's what tiers are for.
- Tiers mean different audiences; write to the tier. A "supporter" post and a "studio" post should feel different.
- **Be honest when things are hard.** Patrons who stick around usually do so because of the relationship, not just the output.

## 3. Web copy

**Headlines:** say what the reader gets, not what the thing is.
- "Prints that ship in one week" not "Our Shipping Policy"
- "Original digital art by an Indigenous artist" not "About This Site"

**CTAs:** use a specific verb with a clear outcome.
- "Buy a print" / "Join the Patreon" / "See all wildlife prints" — not "Learn more" or "Click here."
- One primary CTA per page section. Two equal CTAs compete with each other and both lose.

**About pages:** lead with what you do and for whom, not your CV. The bio can follow; the hook can't.

**Product / artwork descriptions:**
- Sensory and specific: material, size, what the image actually shows and why it exists.
- Story earns the price. A paragraph about what the piece means is more persuasive than a list of dimensions.
- End with what happens next (buy, add to cart, see related work) — don't make them hunt.

**Length:** short. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it. Web readers scan before they read; earn the read first.

**What to avoid:**
- "Welcome to my website" — they're already here
- Passive voice on action pages ("prints can be purchased" → "buy a print")
- Vague qualifiers ("very unique," "truly special") — they signal the thing isn't speaking for itself

---

## 4. Checklist

- [ ] First sentence earns the second; no preamble
- [ ] One idea, one CTA, one ask per unit of content
- [ ] Concrete details — names, numbers, specifics — not abstractions
- [ ] Voice consistent with the rest of your writing
- [ ] Platform format respected (alt text, CWs, caption structure)
- [ ] Cut anything that can be cut without losing meaning
