# photography-fundamentals

Photography is the practice of finding light and deciding what to do with it. The technical controls are the vocabulary; seeing is the skill. This covers the fundamentals of digital photography — how the camera works, how light behaves, and how to use both intentionally.

---

## The exposure triangle

Three controls determine exposure. They interact — changing one requires adjusting another to maintain the same overall brightness.

### Aperture (f-stop)

The opening in the lens that lets light in. Measured in f-stops — counterintuitively, smaller numbers mean wider openings.

| f-stop | Opening | Depth of field | Use |
|--------|---------|---------------|-----|
| f/1.4–f/2.8 | Wide | Shallow (soft background) | Portraits, subject isolation, low light |
| f/4–f/5.6 | Medium | Moderate | General purpose, wildlife |
| f/8–f/11 | Narrow | Deep (front-to-back sharp) | Landscapes, architecture |
| f/16+ | Very narrow | Very deep | Studio, maximum depth of field |

Depth of field also depends on focal length and distance to subject — a 200mm lens at f/5.6 blurs backgrounds far more than a 35mm at f/5.6.

### Shutter speed

How long the sensor is exposed to light. Controls motion — both subject motion and camera shake.

| Speed | Use |
|-------|-----|
| 1/2000s+ | Freeze fast action — birds in flight, lions |
| 1/500–1/1000s | Wildlife, sports |
| 1/125–1/250s | General handheld |
| 1/60s | Minimum for most lenses without image stabilization |
| 1/30s and slower | Tripod needed; motion blur in moving subjects |
| 1s+ | Long exposure — light trails, water smoothing, stars |

Rule of thumb for handheld: minimum shutter speed ≈ 1 / (focal length in mm). At 200mm, use at least 1/200s; with stabilization you can push slower.

### ISO

The sensor's sensitivity to light. Higher ISO = brighter image but more digital noise (grain).

| ISO | Character |
|-----|-----------|
| 100–400 | Clean, fine detail |
| 800–1600 | Acceptable noise in most cameras |
| 3200–6400 | Visible noise; still usable depending on subject and output size |
| 12800+ | Heavy noise; use only when there's no alternative |

Modern full-frame cameras handle ISO 3200–6400 well. Crop sensor cameras show more noise at equivalent ISOs. Don't fear high ISO — a sharp photo with noise beats a blurry clean one.

## Understanding light

Light quality and direction matter more than quantity.

### Quality

**Hard light** — direct sun, bare flash. Creates strong shadows with sharp edges. Good for dramatic effect; unflattering for many subjects; works well for texture.

**Soft light** — overcast sky, light through a large window, bounced flash. Shadows are gradual and gentle. Easier to work with, more flattering for most subjects.

**Golden hour** — the hour after sunrise and before sunset. Light is warm, directional, and soft. Long shadows add depth. Most landscape and nature photographers structure their day around it.

**Blue hour** — 20–30 minutes before sunrise and after sunset. The sky is deep blue, ambient light is very low. Works beautifully for cityscapes, water reflections, and architectural shots.

### Direction

- **Front light** (behind the camera) — even illumination, little shadow, can look flat
- **Side light** — emphasizes texture and dimension; great for landscapes and detail shots
- **Back light** — subject between camera and light source; creates rim lighting, silhouettes, or dramatic glow; exposes for the subject not the sky
- **Top light** (midday sun) — harsh, unflattering; generally the worst time for most subjects

### Working with unusual light

**UV and colored light** (jellyfish tanks, neon, aquariums): set white balance manually or shoot RAW to correct later. Meter on the subject — the environment will often be very bright or very dark by comparison. Use a wide aperture and higher ISO; subjects typically can't be lit any other way.

**White subjects on dark backgrounds** (swans, egrets on dark water): the camera's meter will overexpose white to make the "average" brightness 18% gray — dial in -1 to -2 stops of exposure compensation, or use spot metering on the subject. Check the histogram; the white should land near the right edge without clipping.

## Composition

Composition is about where you place things in the frame and what you include or exclude.

### Core principles

**Rule of thirds** — divide the frame into a 3×3 grid; place subjects and horizons on the lines and intersections rather than dead center. It's a starting point, not a law.

**Leading lines** — roads, canals, fences, rivers guide the eye through the frame. Use them to pull the viewer toward the subject.

**Foreground interest** — including something in the near foreground (a boat, rocks, flowers) adds depth and grounds the viewer in the scene.

**Negative space** — empty space around a subject lets it breathe and can convey isolation, calm, or scale.

**Frame within a frame** — doorways, arches, branches framing the subject add depth and focus attention.

**Fill the frame** — for wildlife and portraits, move closer (or use a longer lens). Unnecessary space dilutes impact.

### Horizon and verticals

Keep horizontal lines level unless you're tilting intentionally. Leaning buildings and tilted horizons read as mistakes unless the angle is dramatic enough to be clearly deliberate. Many cameras have a level indicator in the viewfinder.

### Perspective and angle

Eye level is the default — and often not the most interesting choice. Get low for wildlife (ground-level shots of birds and animals feel immediate). Shoot from above for pattern and abstraction. Move your feet before you zoom.

## Focusing

**Single-point AF** — you choose where the camera focuses. Best for still subjects when you want precise control.

**Continuous AF (AI Servo on Canon, AF-C on Nikon/Sony)** — camera tracks a moving subject. Essential for wildlife and action.

**Eye-detection AF** — modern cameras can lock onto and track eyes of people and animals. Use it when your camera has it; it's very good for portraits and wildlife at moderate distances.

**Manual focus** — for macro, low contrast scenes, and situations where AF hunts (dark, backlit, subject behind glass).

**Back-button focus** — separate the AF activation from the shutter button. The shutter fires without refocusing; the AF thumb button tracks movement. Many sports and wildlife photographers use this as their default.

## RAW vs JPEG

**JPEG**: processed in-camera, compressed, smaller files. What you see is what you get — limited latitude in post-processing.

**RAW**: unprocessed sensor data, larger files, requires post-processing. Far more latitude to recover highlights, lift shadows, adjust white balance, without quality loss.

Shoot RAW if:
- You're doing any serious post-processing
- Lighting is difficult (high contrast, mixed sources)
- White balance might be off
- You want to recover detail in highlights or shadows

The jellyfish photo above was almost certainly shot in conditions where RAW gave you the ability to pull detail from the translucent bell and the dark background without blowing either out.

## Reading the histogram

The histogram shows the distribution of tones from black (left) to white (right). Spikes hard against either edge mean clipping — pure black with no detail, or pure white with no detail.

```
Underexposed    Ideal           Overexposed
|                             |
|█                        █   |     |           █            |     |                       █|
|██████                    |     |        ████████        |     |              ████████|
└────────────────────────────     └────────────────────────────     └────────────────────────────
 L                          H      L                          H      L                          H
```

"Expose to the right" — push exposure as far right as you can without clipping highlights. This maximizes the data the sensor captures, giving more to work with in post, especially for shadow detail.

For scenes with bright whites (snow, swans), slight right-side proximity to the wall is expected — the key is not clipping. Enable the highlight warning ("blinkies") on your camera to see overexposed areas in the preview.

## White balance

White balance tells the camera what color is "white" under the current light.

| Setting | Use |
|---------|-----|
| Auto (AWB) | Works well in most situations; shoot RAW and fix in post if off |
| Daylight | Outdoor sun, ~5500K |
| Cloudy | Slightly warmer than daylight; good on overcast days |
| Shade | Warm — shade is cooler light, this compensates |
| Tungsten | Very warm indoors; corrects orange cast from incandescent bulbs |
| Flash | Matches electronic flash color |
| Custom / Kelvin | Full manual control; use when precision matters |

If shooting RAW, white balance can be changed completely in post with no quality loss. Set AWB and fix it in Lightroom.

## Wildlife and nature photography

### Get close without getting close

Long lenses (200mm, 400mm, 600mm) let you fill the frame from a distance, which is critical for wildlife that flushes when you approach. A 100–400mm zoom is the most versatile wildlife lens.

### Be patient and be still

Wildlife photography is 80% waiting. Find a good position, get comfortable, minimize movement and noise, and let the animals come to you or return to normal behavior. The interesting moments happen when the animal forgets you're there.

### Pre-focus on a spot

For predictable action (a bird returning to a nest, an animal at a watering hole), pre-focus on where the action will happen rather than trying to acquire focus when it starts.

### Shutter priority for moving subjects

In variable light, use Shutter Priority (Tv/S mode) to lock in a fast enough shutter speed, and let ISO and aperture float. Set Auto ISO with a minimum shutter speed in your camera's auto ISO settings — many cameras support this now.

### Background matters

A busy background distracts. A wide aperture blurs it; a low angle often puts sky or water behind the subject instead of clutter.

## Post-processing workflow (Lightroom / Adobe Camera Raw)

A consistent workflow prevents you from making the same decisions twice:

1. **Import** — rename files, apply a baseline preset on import (exposure, clarity, sharpening defaults you like)
2. **Cull** — flag keepers, reject obvious failures. Don't process rejects.
3. **Global adjustments** — exposure, highlights/shadows, whites/blacks, white balance, contrast
4. **Local adjustments** — graduated filters (darken sky), radial filters (lighten subject), brush for spot corrections
5. **Noise reduction and sharpening** — especially for high-ISO shots; Lightroom's AI noise reduction is excellent
6. **Export** — JPEG for sharing/web (sRGB, 72–150 DPI, resize for output), TIFF for print (Adobe RGB, 300 DPI)

Non-destructive editing: Lightroom never modifies the original RAW file. Your edits are stored as instructions. You can always revert to the original.

## Documenting your own artwork

Photographing flat artwork requires consistent, controlled conditions:

- **Two lights at 45°** on either side of the work, same distance and power — eliminates hotspots and shadows
- **Camera parallel to the artwork** — any angle creates keystoning (trapezoidal distortion). Use a level.
- **Custom white balance** off a gray card or white paper under your lights
- **f/8–f/11** for maximum sharpness across the flat plane
- **Tripod** — mandatory
- **Shoot tethered** if possible so you can check on a large screen

For textured work (heavy media, sculptural pieces), directional side lighting reveals texture better than flat front lighting.
