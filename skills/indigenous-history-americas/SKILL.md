# Indigenous History of the Americas

## Scale and diversity first

Before anything else: Indigenous peoples of the Americas are not one people. At the time of European contact there were hundreds of distinct nations, languages, cultures, economies, and political systems across two continents — from the Arctic to Tierra del Fuego. Estimates of pre-contact population range from 50 to 100 million people. That diversity persists today. There is no single "Indian experience," no monolithic culture, no unified worldview. Generalizations are almost always wrong.

---

## Pre-contact: civilizations and complexity

European colonizers arrived in worlds that were neither empty nor "primitive."

**Mesoamerica**
- **Maya** — one of the most sophisticated civilizations on earth. Mathematics (including zero), astronomy, a fully developed writing system, monumental architecture, complex city-states. Classic period 250–900 CE; still very much alive today with millions of Maya people in Guatemala and Mexico.
- **Aztec / Mexica** — the Aztec Empire at its height (15th–early 16th century) controlled much of central Mexico, with Tenochtitlán (modern Mexico City) as its capital — a city larger than any in contemporary Europe.

**South America**
- **Inca / Tawantinsuyu** — the largest empire in pre-Columbian America, stretching 4,000 miles along the Andes. Sophisticated road systems, agricultural terracing, quipu record-keeping, massive stonework without mortar.

**North America**
- **Mississippian culture** — large mound-building civilization centered in the Mississippi River valley. Cahokia (near modern St. Louis) had a population of 10,000–20,000 at its peak around 1100 CE — larger than London at the time.
- **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy** — the Six Nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora). A sophisticated democratic confederacy that influenced the framers of the U.S. Constitution.
- **Ancestral Puebloans** — builders of Chaco Canyon and Mesa Verde. Complex multi-story architecture, trade networks spanning thousands of miles.
- **Pacific Northwest** — rich cultures built on salmon abundance: potlatch ceremonies, totem poles, complex social hierarchies, sophisticated art traditions.
- **Plains nations** — the horse cultures of the Great Plains (Lakota, Comanche, Cheyenne, etc.) are relatively recent, emerging after the Spanish reintroduction of horses in the 17th century.

## Contact, colonization, and catastrophe

### The Doctrine of Discovery
The legal and theological framework that justified European colonization. Papal bulls (1452, 1493) declared that Christian explorers could claim lands inhabited by non-Christians. This doctrine was incorporated into U.S. law in *Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823) and has never been fully repudiated. It is the legal foundation of most U.S. land claims — and Indigenous activists have called for its repudiation for decades.

### Population collapse
The primary mechanism of colonization was disease. Indigenous peoples had no immunity to smallpox, measles, influenza, and other European diseases. Population declined by an estimated 50–90% in the first century after contact — one of the largest demographic catastrophes in human history. Some nations were entirely wiped out before European settlers ever saw them.

### Conquest and enslavement
- Spanish colonization of the Caribbean and Americas involved mass enslavement, forced labor (the encomienda system), and deliberate violence. The population of Hispaniola collapsed from an estimated 250,000 to near zero within 50 years of Columbus's arrival.
- The conquest of Mexico (1519–1521) and Peru (1532) destroyed the Aztec and Inca empires.
- Enslavement of Indigenous peoples in North America preceded and ran parallel to the African slave trade.

## U.S. history: dispossession and policy

### Treaty era
The U.S. government signed over 370 treaties with Indigenous nations between 1778 and 1871. Every single one was broken, abrogated, or reinterpreted by the U.S. to the disadvantage of Indigenous peoples. Treaties are still legally binding — they are "the supreme law of the land" under the Constitution — and treaty rights remain live legal and political issues today.

### Indian Removal Act (1830)
Andrew Jackson's policy of forcibly removing Native nations from the Southeast to west of the Mississippi. The Cherokee Nation challenged it all the way to the Supreme Court and won (*Worcester v. Georgia*, 1832). Jackson refused to enforce the ruling. The result was the **Trail of Tears** — the forced march of the Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, and Seminole nations, during which thousands died.

### Indian Wars
A series of military campaigns from the colonial period through the late 19th century aimed at dispossessing Indigenous peoples of their land. Key moments:
- **Sand Creek Massacre** (1864) — Colorado militia slaughtered a peaceful Cheyenne and Arapaho encampment. Roughly 150–200 people killed, mostly women, children, and elderly.
- **Wounded Knee** (1890) — U.S. Army killed between 250–300 Lakota people, including many women and children. 20 soldiers received the Medal of Honor, which Indigenous activists have called to be rescinded.

### Boarding schools / residential schools
Perhaps the most deliberate and sustained cultural genocide in U.S. and Canadian history. From the 1870s through the 1970s, Indigenous children were forcibly removed from their families and sent to government and church-run boarding schools. The explicit goal, stated by Richard Henry Pratt who founded the first school: "Kill the Indian, save the man." Children were forbidden to speak their languages, practice their religions, or maintain contact with their families. Physical, emotional, and sexual abuse was widespread. Children died in large numbers — from disease, abuse, and neglect — and were often buried in unmarked graves on school grounds. In 2021, ground-penetrating radar began finding hundreds of unmarked graves at former Canadian residential school sites. The U.S. Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative (2022) identified 53 burial sites at 19 schools in the U.S.

### Allotment Era (Dawes Act, 1887)
Broke up communally held reservation lands into individual allotments, with "surplus" land sold to white settlers. Indigenous landholding went from 138 million acres in 1887 to 48 million acres by 1934 — a loss of 90 million acres in 47 years.

### Termination and Relocation (1940s–1960s)
Federal policy to end the government-to-government relationship with tribes, terminate federal recognition, and relocate Native people from reservations to cities. 109 tribes were terminated. Urban relocation brought large Indigenous populations to cities like Chicago, Los Angeles, Minneapolis, and Denver — which is why urban Indigenous communities are significant today.

### American Indian Movement (AIM)
Founded in Minneapolis in 1968, initially to address police brutality against urban Native people. AIM became the center of Red Power activism:
- **Occupation of Alcatraz** (1969–1971) — 89 Native activists occupied the island under the Ft. Laramie Treaty's provision for unused federal land.
- **Trail of Broken Treaties** (1972) — caravan to Washington DC, occupation of the Bureau of Indian Affairs.
- **Wounded Knee** (1973) — 71-day occupation of the town of Wounded Knee on the Pine Ridge Reservation, demanding treaty rights and investigating corruption.

Key figures: Dennis Banks, Russell Means, Clyde and Vernon Bellecourt, Mary Crow Dog, Leonard Peltier (imprisoned since 1977 in a case widely considered a miscarriage of justice).

### Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 1978)
Passed in response to the systematic removal of Native children from their families by state welfare agencies — by the 1970s, 25–35% of all Native children had been separated from their families and placed with non-Native families or institutions. ICWA established standards requiring placement with family, tribal members, or other Native families. It has been under sustained legal attack; the Supreme Court upheld it in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023).

## Key concepts

**Sovereignty** — Indigenous nations are sovereign governments with a government-to-government relationship with the United States. This is not a metaphor or a courtesy — it is a legal reality established by treaties, statutes, and court decisions. Tribal sovereignty means tribes have the right to govern themselves, make laws, and maintain cultural practices.

**Federal recognition** — the U.S. government officially recognizes 574 tribal nations. Recognition determines eligibility for federal programs and services and establishes the government-to-government relationship. Many nations are unrecognized; the process for recognition is lengthy and often unjust.

**Blood quantum** — a colonial-era concept requiring individuals to prove a specific percentage of "Indian blood" to be considered Native. Most tribes require members to prove enrollment-eligible ancestry; some use blood quantum minimums. It is widely criticized as a tool of erasure — mathematically, it tends toward zero over generations.

**Trust responsibility** — the U.S. government's legal obligation to protect Indigenous peoples' lands, assets, and rights, stemming from treaties and statutes. It is frequently violated.

**MMIWG** — Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls. Indigenous women are murdered at rates more than 10 times the national average in some areas. This crisis is the result of intersecting factors including jurisdictional gaps in law enforcement on tribal lands, historical trauma, and ongoing structural racism.

**Land back** — a contemporary movement calling for the return of Indigenous lands to Indigenous peoples. Not universally defined — ranges from literal land return to broader concepts of Indigenous stewardship, governance, and sovereignty over ancestral territories.

## Contemporary Indigenous life

Indigenous peoples are not historical — they are present. Today:
- 574 federally recognized tribal nations in the U.S.
- Approximately 9.7 million people identify as American Indian or Alaska Native alone or in combination (2020 Census)
- Large urban Indigenous populations in cities across the U.S.
- Ongoing cultural revitalization: language reclamation, traditional arts, ceremony
- Ongoing political struggles: water rights (Standing Rock, 2016), land rights, treaty enforcement, MMIW, boarding school accountability
- Growing Indigenous representation in arts, literature, film, politics, and scholarship

## Essential reading

**History and politics**
- Vine Deloria Jr. — *Custer Died for Your Sins* (1969) — the foundational text of modern Indigenous political thought; still essential
- Vine Deloria Jr. — *God Is Red* — Indigenous spirituality vs. Western religion and land ethics
- Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz — *An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States* — comprehensive, unflinching
- Dee Brown — *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* — the Indian Wars from Indigenous perspectives
- Nick Estes — *Our History Is the Future* — Standing Rock in the long context of Lakota resistance
- Mishuana Goeman — *Mark My Words: Native Women Mapping Our Nations*

**Literature and memoir**
- N. Scott Momaday — *House Made of Dawn* (first Native American Pulitzer winner, 1969)
- Leslie Marmon Silko — *Ceremony*, *Almanac of the Dead*
- Louise Erdrich — *Love Medicine*, *The Night Watchman* (Pulitzer 2021)
- Sherman Alexie — *The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven*
- Tommy Orange — *There There* (urban Native experience)
- Mary Crow Dog — *Lakota Woman*
- James Welch — *Fools Crow*, *Winter in the Blood*
- Joy Harjo (Muscogee) — poet, U.S. Poet Laureate 2019–2022

**Contemporary scholarship**
- Kim TallBear — *Native American DNA* (on blood quantum and genetics)
- Dina Gilio-Whitaker — *As Long As Grass Grows* (Indigenous environmentalism)
- David Treuer — *The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* (counters *Bury My Heart*, argues for resilience and continuity)

**Film**
- *Dawnland* (2018) — Maine's truth and reconciliation commission on the boarding school era
- *Gather* (2020) — Indigenous food sovereignty
- *Reservation Dogs* (FX, 2021–2023) — contemporary reservation life, largely Indigenous-written and directed
- *Rutherford Falls* (2021–2022) — tribal sovereignty and gaming, co-created by Sierra Teller Ornelas (Navajo)
- *Smoke Signals* (1998) — first feature film written, directed, and co-produced by Native Americans

## What Claude should keep in mind

- **Present tense** — Indigenous cultures, languages, and nations are alive. Avoid framing them as past or vanishing.
- **Nation specificity** — when possible, name the specific nation rather than using "Native American" or "Indigenous" as a catchall.
- **Diversity** — what is true of the Lakota may not be true of the Navajo. What is true in Oklahoma may not be true in Montana.
- **Sovereignty matters** — Indigenous nations are governments, not ethnic groups. This distinction shapes law, policy, and how people understand their own identity.
- **The personal is political** — for Indigenous people, culture, language, ceremony, and daily life are inseparable from political struggle. Art is resistance. Language is resistance. Existence is resistance.
