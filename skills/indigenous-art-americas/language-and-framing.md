## 1. Naming

**No universal term is universally preferred.** Ask when possible; use specificity when possible.

| Term | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **Indigenous** | Broadly used internationally; covers all Indigenous peoples globally; often preferred in academic and advocacy contexts |
| **Native American** | Common in US contexts; US government legal/administrative term; widely used by Native people themselves |
| **American Indian** | Preferred by many Native people, especially older generations; used in law (Indian Country, Indian Arts and Crafts Act); some find it outdated |
| **First Nations** | Preferred in Canada for First Nations peoples (not Métis or Inuit, who have their own terms) |
| **First Peoples** | Broader Canadian usage covering First Nations, Métis, and Inuit |
| **Aboriginal** | Used in Canada and Australia; falling out of favor in Canada in favor of "Indigenous" |
| **Alaska Native** | Correct for Indigenous peoples of Alaska (Inuit, Yup'ik, Athabascan, Aleut/Unangan, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, and others) |
| **Tribal nation / nation** | Accurate; emphasizes political sovereignty rather than ethnicity |

**Specific nation names when known are always better than general terms.** "Diné" rather than "Navajo" (Navajo is a Spanish/Pueblo loan word, though many Diné use it themselves). "Anishinaabe" rather than "Chippewa" or "Ojibwe" (though both are used). "Haudenosaunee" rather than "Iroquois" (though Iroquois remains in common use). Check what a community uses for itself.

**Avoid pan-tribal generalizations.** "Native Americans believe…" or "Indigenous art always…" flattens the diversity of hundreds of distinct cultures. Be specific about which nation or region you're describing.

## 2. Terms to avoid

For a more detailed professional-context catalog — severity ratings, historical origins, and alternatives — see [indigenous-bias-awareness / terms-and-phrases](../indigenous-bias-awareness/terms-and-phrases.md).

| Avoid | Why | Instead |
|-------|-----|---------|
| **Primitive** | Evolutionary framing that positions Indigenous cultures as precursors to modernity | Describe the tradition specifically |
| **Savage** | Colonial dehumanization; still appears in older scholarship | — |
| **Tribal** (used dismissively) | As an adjective implying lesser complexity | Nation-specific terms; "from the [Nation] tradition" |
| **Ancient/vanishing** (as framing) | Implies Indigenous peoples and traditions are past-tense | Many traditions are living; be specific about historical vs. contemporary |
| **Squaw** | Slur; has been removed from many US place names | Nation-specific terms |
| **Chief** (generically) | Specific political title flattened into stereotype; not all nations have "chiefs" in any meaningful sense | The specific title in the specific nation's language when known |
| **Totem pole** (applied outside Northwest Coast) | Totem poles are specific to particular Northwest Coast nations; the term is often misapplied | Only use for Northwest Coast carved poles |
| **Warpaint** | Reduces complex ceremonial and identity practices to a warfare cliché | Body painting, face painting, ceremonial design |
| **Spirit animal** | A New Age appropriation of a specific concept that varies significantly across nations; broadly not how most nations describe animal relationships | The specific concept in the specific tradition (clan animal, guardian spirit, etc.) |
| **Powwow** (used casually) | A specific ceremonial and social gathering; using it as slang for any meeting is disrespectful | — |
| **Indian** (as adjective for things) | "Indian giver," "Indian summer" — these are slurs embedded in idiom | — |

## 3. Framing pitfalls

**"Pre-Columbian" centers Columbus.** The art didn't become pre-contact because Columbus arrived; it had its own history and periodization. "Pre-contact" is somewhat better; even better is simply naming the period and culture accurately ("Classic Maya," "Late Woodland," "Inca imperial period").

**The "frozen in time" problem.** Museum labels and popular coverage often present Indigenous art as if it stopped at contact or at some fixed historical moment. Most traditions are living and evolving. Using the present tense for living traditions and being specific about historical vs. contemporary contexts matters.

**The "craft" vs. "art" hierarchy.** Western art history's hierarchy — painting and sculpture as fine art, textiles and ceramics as craft — has been applied to Indigenous traditions in ways that systematically undervalue their most sophisticated media. A Diné weaving encoding cosmological knowledge is not "craft" in a lesser sense.

**Authenticity as purity.** The assumption that Indigenous art is only "authentic" when it uses pre-contact materials and techniques and has no European influence is both ahistorical and patronizing. All traditions evolve through contact with other cultures. Trade beads became central to traditions that are now centuries old. Silver was adopted and transformed by the Diné and Mapuche. Contact and innovation don't make a tradition inauthentic.

**"Discovered by" and Western-centered attribution.** Maria Martinez is sometimes described as having "revived" Pueblo pottery, which centers her relationship with market and collectors rather than the continuous tradition she came from. Norval Morrisseau is sometimes credited with "inventing" Woodland painting rather than drawing from existing Anishinaabe visual and ceremonial traditions.

**Sacred vs. public.** Some knowledge, imagery, and objects are restricted — sacred, ceremonially significant, or belonging to specific clan or society. Not all knowledge about a tradition is appropriate to discuss, share, or display. When in doubt about whether something is publicly shareable, don't share it. Museum collections often contain sacred objects that should not be publicly displayed; ongoing repatriation processes address this.

## 4. Decolonial concepts useful for conversation

**Repatriation** — the return of cultural objects, human remains, and knowledge to Indigenous communities. NAGPRA (1990) in the US; ongoing internationally. Major cases: Elgin Marbles (not Indigenous but same logic), Benin Bronzes, Moctezuma's headdress, Maori taonga. Many objects in major museum collections have contested status.

**NAGPRA** — Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (US, 1990). Requires federal agencies and institutions receiving federal funding to return Native American cultural items and human remains to lineal descendants and culturally affiliated tribes. Implementation has been slow; the Smithsonian has been particularly criticized.

**Appropriation vs. appreciation** — a distinction that matters but is often oversimplified. Appropriation involves taking cultural elements without permission or context, often for profit or aesthetics, often by members of a dominant culture from a marginalized one. The line is not always obvious but the concept is not infinitely fuzzy: using headdresses as costume is appropriation; engaging respectfully with another culture's art and understanding its context is different.

**Blood quantum** — a colonial classification system imposed by the US government requiring Indigenous people to prove a certain percentage of Indigenous ancestry to be legally recognized. Now used by some tribes for membership but widely critiqued as a tool of elimination. Relevant in debates about who can legally sell art as "Native American" under the Indian Arts and Crafts Act.

**Survivance** — (again, because it's important) Gerald Vizenor's term for the active, creative presence and resistance of Indigenous peoples — not mere survival but an assertion of continued existence and vitality against narratives of disappearance and defeat. Much contemporary Indigenous art is explicitly survivance.

**Land as relational** — in many Indigenous worldviews, land is not property or resource but a network of relationships with obligations on both sides. Art that engages with land (land art, place-based work, landscape painting) in Indigenous contexts often carries this relational meaning rather than the Western romantic or aesthetic framing of landscape.

## 5. For conversations

When discussing Indigenous art:
- Lead with the specific nation or culture when known; avoid generalizing across nations.
- Use present tense for living traditions.
- Be willing to say "I'm not certain" rather than guessing at ceremonial or sacred significance.
- Distinguish between what is publicly documented and what is restricted knowledge.
- Acknowledge when something is contested or debated within Indigenous communities — these conversations are live.
- Center Indigenous voices, critics, and scholars rather than outside observers when citing critical frameworks.
