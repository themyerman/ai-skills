# Contemporary Indigenous art of the Americas

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

---

## 1. Context

Contemporary Indigenous art did not emerge from a vacuum — it emerged from active suppression (residential and boarding schools, bans on ceremony, forced assimilation policies) and from resistance to that suppression. The explosion of contemporary Indigenous art from the 1960s onward is inseparable from political self-determination movements.

Key moments:
- **American Indian Movement (AIM), founded 1968** — political movement that changed the cultural landscape; many artists were directly connected.
- **Indian Arts and Crafts Act (US, 1990)** — made it illegal to market work as "Native American" without tribal citizenship. A response to widespread market fraud; also a source of ongoing debate about who defines authenticity.
- **UNDRIP (2007)** — UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples; provided international framing for cultural rights including repatriation.
- **Land Back movement (ongoing)** — explicitly connected to contemporary art and curatorial practice.

## 2. Institutions

**United States:**
- **Institute of American Indian Arts (IAIA), Santa Fe** — founded 1962, the most significant institution for contemporary Native art education. Alumni include T.C. Cannon, Jaune Quick-to-See Smith, and many others.
- **Heard Museum, Phoenix** — major collection and exhibition program for Southwest and broader Indigenous art.
- **National Museum of the American Indian (NMAI), Washington DC and New York** — part of the Smithsonian; significant contemporary programming alongside historical collections. Controversially, its curatorial model is community-centered (tribal nations have significant control over how their objects are presented).
- **Eiteljorg Museum, Indianapolis** — significant contemporary Native art collection and fellowship program.
- **First Peoples Worldwide** and various tribal cultural centers.

**Canada:**
- **Winnipeg Art Gallery / Qaumajuq** — world's largest public collection of Inuit art.
- **Vancouver Art Gallery** — significant First Nations programming, particularly Northwest Coast.
- **UBC Museum of Anthropology, Vancouver** — major collections and working relationship with Northwest Coast nations.

**Mexico / Latin America:**
- **Museo Nacional de Antropología, Mexico City** — largest collection of pre-contact Mexican art; framing has been critiqued for nationalist rather than Indigenous perspectives.
- Various national museums in Peru, Guatemala, Bolivia with significant pre-contact and contemporary holdings.

## 3. Key artists (North America)

This is necessarily partial. These are artists significant to the development or current state of the field.

**Historical / foundational:**
- **Norval Morrisseau** (Anishinaabe, 1932–2007) — founder of the Woodland school / Legend painting. Drew on birchbark scroll traditions and Midewiwin ceremony; his paintings opened a path for other Indigenous artists to work from within their own visual traditions rather than assimilating to Western modernism. Called "Copper Thunderbird" by his community.
- **T.C. Cannon** (Caddo/Kiowa, 1946–1978) — painter and poet; studied at IAIA; his work engaged directly with Pop Art while centering Indigenous subjects and critiquing the conditions of Native life.
- **Fritz Scholder** (Luiseño, 1937–2005) — contested figure; explicitly rejected the "Indian artist" label while painting Indians; brought a raw, expressionist energy to Indigenous subjects; his influence on subsequent generations is enormous and his legacy is genuinely debated within Native art communities.
- **Oscar Howe** (Yanktonai Dakota, 1915–1983) — pioneered the move away from the "flat" style that had been imposed on Native artists by patrons; his cubist-influenced work was initially rejected by jurors who said it wasn't "Indian" enough, and his response letter is a foundational document of Native artistic self-determination.
- **Maria Martinez** (San Ildefonso Pueblo, 1887–1980) — developed the matte-on-black pottery style with her husband Julian; a central figure in the market for Pueblo pottery.

**Mid-career to established:**
- **Jaune Quick-to-See Smith** (Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes, b. 1940) — painter and printmaker; major figure in positioning Indigenous art in contemporary critical discourse; her work engages directly with land, sovereignty, and representation.
- **Kay WalkingStick** (Cherokee, b. 1935) — diptych paintings combining abstraction with landscape; significant figure in bringing Native perspective to mainstream contemporary art contexts.
- **James Luna** (Luiseño/Diegueno, 1950–2018) — performance artist whose work (including "The Artifact Piece," in which he placed himself in a museum case) directly critiqued the museological treatment of Native people.
- **Hulleah Tsinhnahjinnie** (Seminole/Muscogee/Diné, b. 1954) — photographer and curator; work engages with historical photography and representation.
- **Rick Bartow** (Wiyot/Mad River Band, 1946–2016) — expressionist painter and sculptor; drawings and paintings dealing with transformation, grief, and the animal world.
- **Marie Watt** (Seneca, b. 1967) — sculptor working with wool blankets as material and metaphor for community, memory, and Indigenous history.
- **Brian Jungen** (Dane-zaa/Swiss, b. 1970) — Vancouver-based; transforms Nike Air Jordans into Northwest Coast-style masks, IKEA furniture into skeletons; work deals with commodity culture, cultural appropriation, and Indigenous identity.

**Younger and mid-career:**
- **Jeffrey Gibson** (Mississippi Choctaw/Cherokee, b. 1972) — paintings, beadwork, and mixed media fusing Plains beadwork traditions with contemporary aesthetics; represented the United States at the Venice Biennale 2024 (first Indigenous artist to do so as a solo representative).
- **Cannupa Hanska Luger** (Mandan/Hidatsa/Arikara/Lakota, b. 1979) — sculpture, performance, video; large-scale community-engaged projects; mirror shields at Standing Rock.
- **Wendy Red Star** (Crow/Apsáalooke, b. 1981) — photography and mixed media; engages with historical photography of Crow people, centering Indigenous perspective in archival material.
- **Andrea Carlson** (Anishinaabe, b. 1979) — painting and drawing; dense, complex imagery engaging with natural history museums, collections, and colonial violence.
- **Steven Paul Judd** (Kiowa/Choctaw) — painter and filmmaker; uses pop culture and humor as Indigenous critique; widely shared visual work.
- **Christi Belcourt** (Métis, b. 1966) — painter working in the Woodland/floral tradition; also a major activist and co-founder of Walking With Our Sisters.
- **Delina White** (Ojibwe) — beadwork artist and advocate for beading as contemporary art.

**Latin America / South America:**
- **Yolanda Gutiérrez** (Zapotec, b. 1970) — installation and sculpture drawing on Zapotec cosmology.
- **Guillermo Gómez-Peña** (Chicano/Mexican, b. 1955) — performance; border culture and hybrid identity.
- Contemporary Shipibo-Konibo artists bringing kené patterns to large-scale installation.
- Living weaving communities in the Andes, Guatemala, and Oaxaca who are both traditional practitioners and contemporary artists.

## 4. Movements and currents

**Native American modernism** — the negotiation between modernist aesthetics and Indigenous identity and tradition. Started mid-20th century; IAIA was central to its development.

**Ledger art revival** — contemporary artists working in the 19th-century ledger art tradition, both as homage and as contemporary political statement.

**Beadwork as fine art** — a significant movement repositioning beadwork from "craft" to contemporary fine art. Artists like Jeffrey Gibson, Wendy Ponca, and many others. The formal precision, conceptual depth, and labor involved in major beadwork pieces makes the "craft" dismissal particularly pointed.

**Indigenous futurism** — term coined by Anishinaabe scholar Grace Dillon; encompasses science fiction, speculative art, and visual work that imagines Indigenous futures rather than only engaging with the past or present of colonial experience. Related to Afrofuturism. Artists including Steven Paul Judd, Cannupa Hanska Luger, and others work in this space.

**Land-based art and Land Back** — art explicitly connected to land sovereignty; cairns, interventions, landscape work. Standing Rock (2016) was a significant catalyst; the mirror shields project by Cannupa Hanska Luger is one example.

**Repatriation and the museum question** — many contemporary Indigenous artists engage directly with museums, collections, and the history of ethnographic extraction. NAGPRA (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990) provides a legal framework for the repatriation of human remains and sacred objects from US institutions; implementation has been contested and slow.

**Digital and new media** — Indigenous artists working in video, digital art, social media, and games. The internet has allowed Indigenous communities to share work, organize, and reach audiences without institutional gatekeepers.

## 5. Critical frameworks

**Survivance** — Gerald Vizenor (Anishinaabe scholar and writer): more than survival, an active, creative presence that resists narratives of disappearance and defeat. Indigenous art as assertion of continued existence.

**Sovereignty** — cultural sovereignty as inseparable from political and land sovereignty. Who controls the representation, exhibition, and interpretation of Indigenous art is a sovereignty question.

**Decolonizing the museum** — critique of how ethnographic and natural history museums have housed Indigenous material as artifact rather than art, as past rather than present, and as property of institutions rather than communities. Many Indigenous curators and artists work explicitly in this space.

**Two-Eyed Seeing** (Etuaptmumk) — Mi'kmaw Elder Albert Marshall's concept of holding Indigenous and Western knowledge systems simultaneously, neither subordinating one to the other. Applied in art contexts to work that draws on both Indigenous tradition and contemporary global art discourse.

**Authenticity debates** — who is "authentically" Indigenous enough to make Indigenous art? The Indian Arts and Crafts Act (1990) uses tribal enrollment as the criterion. Many artists and scholars critique this for reinforcing colonial blood quantum logic. The debate is live and unresolved.

**Craft vs. art** — the Western hierarchy that places painting and sculpture above textiles, ceramics, and beadwork has been specifically damaging to Indigenous art, where the most sophisticated traditional media are often textiles, pottery, and beadwork. Dismantling this hierarchy is an ongoing project.
