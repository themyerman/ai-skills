## Foundational and historical figures

### Norval Morrisseau (Anishinaabe / Bingwi Neyaashi Anishinaabek, 1932–2007)
Called "Copper Thunderbird" within his community, Morrisseau is the founder of the Woodland school (also called Legend painting or Pictographic painting). He developed a style rooted in Anishinaabe birchbark scroll imagery and Midewiwin ceremonial iconography — bold black outlines, x-ray-style figures revealing internal organs and spirit lines, forms that flow into and through each other. He had no formal Western art training and broke a significant cultural rule by making sacred imagery visible outside ceremony; this was both controversial within his community and transformative for Indigenous art broadly.

He was the first Indigenous artist to have a solo show at the National Gallery of Canada (1975). His work demonstrated that Indigenous visual traditions could anchor a contemporary fine art practice on their own terms, without assimilation to European styles. His influence on subsequent generations of Woodland and Anishinaabe artists is enormous.

**Legacy note:** Fake Morrisseaus are a major ongoing scandal — thousands of forgeries entered the market, some facilitated by dealers. The authentication problem remains unresolved and is itself a significant story in Canadian Indigenous art.

---

### Fritz Scholder (Luiseño, 1937–2005)
Scholder is one of the most contested figures in Native American art history, and the debate around him is useful to understand. He was mixed Luiseño and German-American, trained at the California College of Arts and taught at the Institute of American Indian Arts (IAIA) in Santa Fe — and he famously declared he was "not an Indian painter" while producing a sustained series of paintings of Indians.

His "Indian" series (1967 onward) took the icons of Native American representation — the war bonnet, the blanket, the plains warrior — and rendered them with a raw, Neo-Expressionist energy that was simultaneously satirical, empathetic, and disturbing. A figure in a feathered headdress eating an ice cream cone. A "Super Indian." An "American Portrait" that refuses dignity or stereotype and offers something stranger. He used the visual language of Bacon, de Kooning, and Hockney — painters he explicitly cited — in the service of Indigenous critique.

The controversy: many Native artists and critics felt his work, whatever its intention, reinforced or aestheticized harmful imagery. Others see him as having blown the door open for Indigenous artists to work with irony, ambiguity, and the full register of contemporary painting. His influence on artists like T.C. Cannon (his student) and subsequent generations is direct.

---

### T.C. Cannon (Caddo/Kiowa, 1946–1978)
Cannon studied under Scholder at IAIA and took his teacher's ironic, Pop-inflected approach in a more personal and politically explicit direction. His paintings center Indigenous figures with a cool, graphic precision — bold flat color, deliberate visual references to Western art history placed against traditional regalia or reservation settings. In *Collector #5* (1975), a Crow man in full regalia sits in an overstuffed chair surrounded by art books and Van Gogh's *Bedroom in Arles* on the wall — a dry inversion of the collector/collected relationship.

He was also a poet and Vietnam veteran, and his work carries the full weight of those experiences. He died at 31 in a car accident, which gives his relatively small body of work an enormous "what if" quality. He is frequently cited as one of the most important figures in 20th-century Native American art.

---

### Oscar Howe (Yanktonai Dakota, 1915–1983)
Howe studied under Dorothy Dunn at the Santa Fe Indian School, which taught a "traditional" flat style that the school's patrons expected and enforced. He pushed against this, developing a cubist-influenced style with dynamic, interlocking figures in vivid color. In 1958, a juror for the Philbrook Museum's Indian Annual rejected his entry for being insufficiently "traditional." His response letter — in which he argued for the right of Native artists to develop their own contemporary practice without being confined to an outsider's idea of authenticity — is a foundational document in the history of Native artistic self-determination.

His influence is largely indirect: he opened a conceptual door that subsequent generations walked through.

## Mid-career to established figures

### Jaune Quick-to-See Smith (Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes, b. 1940)
One of the most significant living Native American artists and one of the most important voices in the field's critical development. Her paintings are large, layered, and demanding — she works in mixed media on canvas, often incorporating collage, text, maps, and newspaper fragments alongside gestural painting. Her imagery ranges from abstracted animal forms to the direct political (her *Trade (Gifts for Trading Land with White People)* canoe, hung with cheap Native American kitsch, is one of the most pointed works of political commentary in contemporary American art).

She co-founded the American Indian Community House Gallery in New York and has spent decades building infrastructure for Indigenous artists and curators. She is a fierce critic of the market's tendency to treat Native art as ethnographic artifact or tourist commodity, and her own work constantly interrogates the terms on which Native artists are received.

A major retrospective, *Memory Map*, opened at the Whitney Museum of American Art in 2023 — long overdue institutional recognition. Her career arc is itself an argument about who gets seen and when.

---

### Danielle SeeWalker (Standing Rock Sioux / Hunkpapa Lakota, contemporary)
SeeWalker is a digital artist and illustrator whose work reclaims the representation of Indigenous women. Her "Native Pin-Up" series takes the retro pin-up format — a visual vocabulary built to objectify — and redirects it entirely: her subjects are Indigenous women depicted with humor, pride, and specificity, wearing regalia and traditional dress, in contexts that center their own gaze and agency. The work is simultaneously accessible (the format is familiar) and pointed (the reversal is complete).

She works primarily in digital illustration and has a significant following on social media, where she uses visibility and humor as tools. Her work exemplifies how digital platforms have allowed Indigenous artists to reach audiences without institutional gatekeepers — and how reclaiming imagery that has been used against Indigenous people is itself an act of cultural sovereignty.

---

### Kay WalkingStick (Cherokee, b. 1935)
WalkingStick is known for her diptych format: two panels placed together, one typically abstract and gestural, the other a landscape — often the same land rendered differently. The pairing creates a dialogue between inner experience and outer place, between Indigenous relationship to land and Western representational conventions of landscape. She has been explicit about the political dimension of her landscape work: land is not neutral territory in Native American experience, and painting it is not a neutral act.

She was one of the first Native American women to achieve significant mainstream art world presence, and her long career (she was still exhibiting actively into her 80s) has made her a persistent counterargument to the art world's habit of treating Indigenous artists as briefly visible rather than sustained presences.

---

### Kent Monkman (Cree, b. 1965)
Monkman's most recognizable work involves large-scale oil paintings that directly reverse the colonial gaze. His alter ego, Miss Chief Eagle Testickle — a provocative, Two-Spirit time-traveling trickster figure played by Monkman himself in photographs and videos — appears in reimagined scenes of colonial contact, replacing the voyeuristic European subjects of 19th-century Western painting with an Indigenous perspective. In his versions, the Indigenous figure has power, agency, humor, and desire.

His twin paintings *Welcoming the Newcomers* and *Resurgence of the People* were commissioned by the Metropolitan Museum of Art in 2019 and installed in the Great Hall — the first time an Indigenous artist had been given that commission. The works depict Indigenous people rescuing dispossessed migrants from a stormy sea, and in the second, Indigenous people reclaiming the museum itself. The audacity of the location was the point.

His work engages simultaneously with colonialism, gender, sexuality, and the history of Western art — specifically the paintings in major museum collections that depict Indigenous people as dying, passive, or noble objects.

---

### Jeffrey Gibson (Mississippi Choctaw/Cherokee, b. 1972)
Gibson's work is immediately recognizable: vibrant, dense, layered — beadwork and textile patterns derived from Plains and Woodland traditions woven together with text, pop culture references, and punk and rave aesthetics. His paintings, sculptures, and beaded garments refuse the separation between "traditional" and "contemporary," between Indigenous visual languages and the full range of the artist's cultural experience.

In 2024 he represented the United States at the Venice Biennale as a solo artist — the first Indigenous person to do so. The pavilion, titled *The Space Between the Lightning and the Thunder*, was a full-environment installation of his collaborative textile and beadwork practice.

---

### Rebecca Belmore (Anishinaabe / Lac Seul First Nation, b. 1960)
Belmore is primarily a performance and installation artist whose work deals with violence against Indigenous women, the body as a site of political resistance, and the specific grief of ongoing crisis. Her performance *Vigil* (2002), held in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside during the period when Robert Pickton was murdering Indigenous women (without, for years, police action), stands as one of the most significant works of public mourning and political witness in Canadian art history.

She represented Canada at the Venice Biennale in 2005. Her installations often use materials that carry weight — water, earth, fabric — and create experiences that are slow, accumulative, and physically felt. She does not aestheticize pain; she makes it present.

---

### Wendy Red Star (Apsáalooke/Crow, b. 1981)
Red Star works with photography, mixed media, and archival research to center Indigenous perspective within material that was originally made to contain or document Indigenous people. Her series *Four Seasons* places her in constructed Plains settings photographed with deliberate artificiality — fake snow, plastic plants — commenting on the staged nature of "authentic" representations of Native life. Her ongoing engagement with 19th-century photographs of Crow delegates and leaders restores names, context, and agency to images that were made for the consuming gaze of ethnography.

She is also a significant educator and has written and spoken about the experience of being a Native woman artist in the contemporary art world.

---

### Cannupa Hanska Luger (Mandan/Hidatsa/Arikara/Lakota, b. 1979)
Luger works at large scale — sculpture, performance, video, and community-engaged projects that involve hundreds of participants. His *Mirror Shield Project* (2016), in which he organized the making and distribution of reflective shields at the Standing Rock water protector camps, turned a community-protection object into an artwork that photographed protestors as they would be seen by law enforcement: as images of themselves. The shields were used; the photographs circulated internationally.

His work consistently blurs the line between art object and tool, between studio practice and political action. He is one of the most visible figures in contemporary Indigenous futurism — his work imagines Indigenous people as active, complex, and future-oriented rather than as subjects of historical narrative.

---

### Nicholas Galanin (Tlingit/Unangan, b. 1979)
Galanin is a conceptual artist, musician, and craftsman trained in both Northwest Coast formal traditions (his family are established Tlingit artists) and contemporary art. His work operates through collision and displacement: Tlingit formal vocabulary appearing in unexpected contexts, Western objects transformed through Indigenous aesthetics, commercial culture examined through Indigenous critical frameworks.

*White Noise, American Prayer Rug* (2018) presents a Navajo rug woven in the silhouette of a prayer rug — two colonized peoples' objects placed in conversation, both reduced by commercial reproduction to surface pattern. His *Indian Children's Bracelet* replicates in silver the metal ID bracelets that residential school children were required to wear. His work demands you look at what is already there.

---

### Gregg Deal (Pyramid Lake Paiute, b. 1975)
Deal is a painter and performance artist whose work confronts the gap between how Indigenous people are imagined and how they actually live. His most well-known performance, *The Last American Indian on Earth*, involves him dressing in stereotypical "traditional" Native costume — headdress, war paint — and walking through public spaces: shopping malls, tourist zones, city streets. The performance captures the public's reactions on video. The piece works because it forces the collision: here is the costume that people expect, applied to a real Indigenous person, doing ordinary things. The discomfort is the content.

His paintings are large-scale and direct — text-heavy, politically explicit, dealing with mascots, media representations, police violence, and the contradictions of Indigenous visibility in American culture. He is also a prolific public voice on social media, using the same confrontational directness his performances employ. He is one of the clearest examples of an Indigenous artist for whom the studio practice and the public argument are continuous.

---

### Christi Belcourt (Métis, b. 1966)
Belcourt paints in a tradition rooted in Métis floral beadwork — dense, intricate patterns of plants, flowers, and animals rendered in acrylic on large canvases. The paintings are meticulous and labor-intensive in a way that deliberately echoes the labor of beading; looking at them requires the same kind of sustained attention that the work demanded to make. They are also political: many of her recent large works deal with Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls (MMIWG) and with environmental destruction.

She is also the co-founder of Walking With Our Sisters, a commemorative art installation for MMIWG that traveled to venues across Canada and the US.

---

### Dallin Maybee (Arapaho, contemporary)
Maybee is a painter and illustrator whose work is grounded in careful, detailed realism applied to Plains Indian subjects — warriors in full regalia, ceremonial figures, historical scenes, and portraits. His paintings convey specificity and dignity: the detail in beadwork, featherwork, and clothing is rendered with the kind of exactness that comes from deep familiarity with the material culture, not decorative approximation. His work has appeared on book covers and album covers as well as in gallery contexts, and he has built a following that spans collectors of fine art and those who come to Indigenous subject matter through music and literature.

Where Scholder and his heirs work through irony and distortion, Maybee works through precision — the figures in his paintings are fully present, not commented upon. The political weight of that choice is real: to paint Arapaho subjects with unflinching beauty and care is its own act of resistance against a century of degraded or sentimentalized imagery.

---

### Brent Learned (Cheyenne/Arapaho, contemporary)
Learned is a painter based in Oklahoma whose work has a distinct and immediately recognizable energy: vivid, saturated color; bold graphic compositions; traditional Plains imagery rendered with a contemporary pop-art sensibility. His warriors, horses, and ceremonial figures pulse with movement — the paintings feel alive in a way that is neither nostalgic nor ethnographic. He is working from inside the tradition, not documenting it from outside.

His paintings occupy an interesting position in the contemporary market: they are accessible (the format is legible, the subjects are recognizable) while being formally ambitious and culturally specific. He has built a significant collector base and a strong social media presence, both of which he uses to keep Indigenous art — and specifically Southern Plains art — visible outside the institutional contexts that have historically controlled who gets seen.

---

### George Curtis Levi (Southern Cheyenne / Southern Arapaho / Oglala Lakota, contemporary)
Levi is a ledger artist who works in the Cheyenne pictographic tradition, using Prismacolor pencils on antique documents — often 19th-century bank checks and other historical papers — as his canvas. The choice of substrate is not incidental: the documents that administered colonial commerce and governance become the surface on which Cheyenne warriors, ceremonies, and victory are depicted. The paper carries its own history; the images Levi places on it carry another.

His work focuses directly on the traumatic history of the Southern Cheyenne and Arapaho — the Sand Creek Massacre, the Washita River attack led by Custer, the Battle of Little Bighorn (Greasy Grass) — rendered from a Cheyenne perspective rather than the settler-historical one. His depictions of the Little Bighorn foreground Cheyenne agency and victory, including figures like Buffalo Calf Road Woman, whose role in the battle has been marginalized in most historical accounts. The work is explicitly oriented toward healing: confronting historical trauma through art rather than leaving it unaddressed.

He is a member of the Cheyenne Kitfox Society, and his practice extends beyond painting to beadwork and painting on rawhide — media with deep roots in Plains material culture. His work has been shown at the National Museum of the American Indian, the Santa Fe Indian Market (SWAIA), and the Heard Museum.

Levi's middle name — Curtis — is itself a pointed fact in the context of his work. That his art centers Cheyenne resistance to the violence associated with Custer's campaigns while carrying a name that echoes the era's cast of characters is not lost on anyone familiar with the history.

## A note on this list

This list is partial and skewed toward artists who have received English-language critical attention. Major traditions and artists in Latin America, the Caribbean, and among Indigenous communities with less market or institutional visibility are underrepresented here — not because they are less significant but because the documentation is less accessible. The [regions-meso-south.md](regions-meso-south.md) file covers some of that ground.

Tom Myer, the person using this skill, is himself an Indigenous digital artist (Cayuga/Haudenosaunee and Ngäbe-Buglé). His lived knowledge and direct relationships in the field take precedence over anything in this file.
