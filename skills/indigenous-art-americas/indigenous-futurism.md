# Indigenous futurism

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

---

## What it is

**Indigenous futurism** is a term coined by Anishinaabe scholar Grace Dillon in her 2012 anthology *Walking the Clouds: An Anthology of Indigenous Science Fiction* (University of Arizona Press). It names a body of creative work — in literature, visual art, film, music, and games — that imagines Indigenous people in the future, in space, in speculative and science-fictional worlds, and in visions of resurgence rather than disappearance.

The term is related to Afrofuturism but is not the same thing and should not be treated as derivative of it. Both emerged from communities for whom standard futures — the optimistic, Western, technological future of mainstream science fiction — have historically either excluded them or positioned them as obstacles to progress. Both reclaim the right to imagine futures. But the specific histories, relationships to land, and conceptual frameworks are distinct.

---

## The foundational reframe

The single most important conceptual move in Indigenous futurism is this:

**For Indigenous peoples, the apocalypse has already happened.**

Colonization — the destruction of populations, languages, lands, and governance systems — was catastrophic on the scale of an apocalypse. Standard post-apocalyptic fiction imagines civilizational collapse as a future event; Indigenous futurism begins from the knowledge that this collapse already occurred, that Indigenous peoples survived it, and that survival itself is a form of futurism.

This reframe changes everything about what Indigenous futurism looks like:
- It is not pessimistic about the future because it has already survived the worst.
- It does not imagine Indigenous people "going back" to a pre-contact past — that past is gone and the living present is what matters.
- It insists on continuity across catastrophe: ancestors are present, traditions are alive, the future is not a break from the past but a continuation of ongoing existence.

Grace Dillon calls this **"Native slipstream"** — a narrative mode in which time is not linear, in which past, present, and future are simultaneous rather than sequential. This is not just a literary device; it reflects actual Indigenous conceptualizations of time in many traditions.

## Relationship to Afrofuturism

Afrofuturism (a term associated with cultural critic Mark Dery, 1993, and developed by scholars including Kodwo Eshun and Alondra Nelson) imagines Black futures and reclaims space, technology, and science fiction for Black experience. Indigenous futurism has clear kinship with this project and the two communities have influenced each other.

Key distinctions:
- **Land** is central to Indigenous futurism in a way it is not to Afrofuturism. For many Indigenous peoples, the relationship to specific land is constitutive of identity, governance, and spirituality. Indigenous futures are often land-based futures — not fantasies of escape to space but visions of regrounded relationship to place.
- **Nation and governance** — Indigenous futurism often engages with sovereignty, treaty rights, and political self-determination in ways specific to the legal and political position of Indigenous peoples.
- **Time** — the non-linear time frameworks prominent in Indigenous futurism are drawn from specific Indigenous philosophical and spiritual traditions, not from a broadly shared Afrodiasporic experience.

They share: the refusal of extinction narratives, the insistence on imagining futures for communities the dominant culture has written off as past-tense, and the use of speculative fiction as a mode of political and cultural resistance.

## Key concepts

**Survivance** (Gerald Vizenor) — active presence and creative resistance rather than mere survival. Indigenous futurism is survivance made visual and narrative.

**Native slipstream** — Grace Dillon's term for the non-linear time characteristic of much Indigenous speculative fiction; ancestors and futures as simultaneously present.

**Resurgence** — the political and cultural project of reviving and reclaiming Indigenous languages, governance, ceremony, and land relationships. Indigenous futurism is often resurgence imagined forward.

**Refusal** — (after anthropologist Audra Simpson's concept of "ethnographic refusal") the choice not to explain, justify, or translate Indigenous experience for a settler audience. Some Indigenous futurist work refuses to center the non-Indigenous reader.

**Technology as Indigenous** — a recurring insistence that technology is not inherently Western or colonial. Many traditions had sophisticated navigation, astronomy, agriculture, and engineering. The smartphone and the social media platform are as available to Indigenous artists as to anyone. The claim that modernity and Indigeneity are incompatible is itself a colonial argument.

## Visual artists

**Cannupa Hanska Luger** (Mandan/Hidatsa/Arikara/Lakota) — large-scale community projects and objects that envision collective Indigenous action. His mirror shields at Standing Rock; ongoing projects imagining Indigenous futures as collective, land-based, and technologically engaged. See [artists.md](artists.md).

**Steven Paul Judd** (Kiowa/Choctaw) — painter, filmmaker, and graphic artist who uses pop culture, humor, and digital tools. His images circulate widely on social media; he deliberately uses the visual language of mass culture (movie posters, superhero aesthetics) to center Indigenous subjects and critique.

**Postcommodity** — an interdisciplinary collective (formerly including Raven Chacon, Cristóbal Martínez, and Kade L. Twist) working across sound, installation, and video. Their work examines the borders — physical, cultural, political — that structure Indigenous experience. *Repellent Fence* (2015), a line of large yellow balloon figures spanning the US/Mexico border, is their best-known work.

**Sonny Assu** (Ligwilda'xw Kwakwaka'wakw, b. 1975) — uses Northwest Coast formal design in commercial and pop contexts. Work includes traditional design applied to cereal boxes (referencing Indian residential school nutritional experiments), and ongoing engagement with colonialism and consumerism through Indigenous visual vocabulary.

**Nep Sidhu** — multimedia artist working with textile, performance, and video; draws on South Asian and Indigenous frameworks in ways that explore non-Western futurisms.

**The Halluci Nation** (formerly A Tribe Called Red) — DJ collective (Bear Witness and 2oolman) whose "Electric Pow Wow" music fuses contemporary electronic dance music with powwow drum and song. Not visual artists in a gallery sense, but their work — and especially Bear Witness's video art — is central to Indigenous futurist visual culture. The combination of ceremonial music and club culture is itself a futurist act: ceremony as alive and contemporary, not past-tense.

## Literature

**Grace Dillon** (Anishinaabe) — *Walking the Clouds* (2012) is the foundational anthology; her introduction is required reading for anyone engaging with Indigenous futurism as a critical concept.

**Eden Robinson** (Haisla/Heiltsuk, b. 1968) — *Son of a Trickster* trilogy; contemporary Indigenous experience inflected with trickster figures, supernatural forces, and lived specificity of Northwest Coast Indigenous community life.

**Cherie Dimaline** (Métis, b. 1975) — *The Marrow Thieves* (2017); dystopian Canada in which Indigenous people are hunted for their bone marrow (which has retained the capacity to dream, which settlers have lost). One of the most read works of Indigenous futurist fiction; direct in its political allegory, devastating in its emotional register.

**Rebecca Roanhorse** (Black/Ohkay Owingeh, b. 1971) — *Trail of Lightning* and the *Between Earth and Sky* series; Navajo Nation in a post-climate apocalypse, trickster figures, monsters from Diné cosmology operating in a near-future world. Also *Star Wars* and *Black Sun* novels. One of the most commercially successful contemporary Indigenous speculative fiction writers.

**Stephen Graham Jones** (Blackfoot, b. 1972) — horror and speculative fiction; *The Only Good Indians* (2020). His work uses horror as a political genre: the monster is often colonialism, the uncanny is the lived experience of Indigenous modernity.

**Darcie Little Badger** (Lipan Apache, b. 1987) — *Elatsoe* and *A Snake Falls to Earth*; draws on Lipan Apache oral tradition and cosmology in YA fantasy contexts.

## Film

**Prey** (2022, directed by Dan Trachtenberg) — a Predator film set in 1719 Comanche territory, with a Comanche hunter as the protagonist, filmed partly in Comanche language. Consulted extensively with the Comanche Nation. Not a perfect film, but its existence — a mainstream action film centered on an Indigenous woman defeating a technologically superior invader — is itself a futurist gesture: it imagines the past differently, which changes what we imagine is possible.

**Blood Quantum** (2019, directed by Jeff Barnaby, Mi'kmaq) — zombie film set on the Mìgmaq reserve of Red Crow, in which Indigenous people are immune to the zombie virus but settlers are not. The reversal is obvious and intentional; the film is both genre exercise and political allegory about immunity, sovereignty, and who gets to survive.

**Driveways / Rhymes for Young Ghouls** — Jeff Barnaby's earlier work, similarly rooted in Mi'kmaq experience and genre conventions.

## Games and interactive media

**Never Alone (Kisima Ingitchuna)** (2014, Upper One Games / E-Line Media, in partnership with the Iñupiaq community) — a puzzle platformer based on an Iñupiaq story, developed in direct collaboration with Cook Inlet Tribal Council elders, storytellers, and community members. Accompanied by documentary footage of Iñupiaq cultural practitioners. One of the first and best examples of an Indigenous community leading the development of a commercial game rooted in their own oral tradition.

**Coyote & Crow** (2021, Coyote & Crow LLC) — a tabletop roleplaying game set in an alternate-history North America where European colonization never occurred. Designed, written, and illustrated primarily by Native American creators, it is as significant for its visual world-building as for its mechanics. The art direction is explicitly Indigenous futurist: cities, technology, fashion, and architecture that look like they emerged organically from Indigenous aesthetic traditions — not Western modernity with Native overlay, but a genuinely distinct civilizational visual vocabulary. Tall cities with design languages drawn from Plains, Southwest, Woodland, and other traditions; technology that is recognizably advanced but not European-derived.

The game was successfully crowdfunded and has become a reference point for what Indigenous futurist world-building looks like in practice. Its existence as a commercial product — with a large Indigenous creative team and significant mainstream uptake — is itself a futurist act: Indigenous people imagining and building the future in a genre (tabletop RPG) that has been almost entirely dominated by European fantasy traditions. The art book and sourcebooks are worth examining as standalone examples of Indigenous futurist visual design.

## How to use this concept in conversation

**Indigenous futurism is not nostalgia.** It is not about returning to a pre-contact past or preserving culture in amber. It is about imagining living, contemporary, future Indigenous people.

**It is not monolithic.** Different nations, communities, and artists have very different visions of what an Indigenous future looks like. There is no single "Indigenous futurism" aesthetic.

**The apocalypse framing matters.** When non-Indigenous people engage with Indigenous futurism without understanding the "already apocalyptic" premise, they tend to read it as simply optimistic or as exotic. Understanding that the genre starts from catastrophe and survival changes how the work reads.

**It is connected to political movements.** Land Back, MMIWG, treaty rights, language revitalization — Indigenous futurism is not separate from these; it is their imaginative dimension.

**It is alive and growing.** The field has expanded significantly since Dillon's 2012 anthology; new writers, artists, and filmmakers are working in this space continuously. It is not a historical phenomenon being studied; it is a living practice.
