## 1. Arctic and Subarctic

**Peoples:** Inuit, Inupiaq, Yup'ik, Iñupiat, Cree, Dene, and many others across Alaska, Canada, and Greenland.

**Key traditions:**
- **Sculpture** in soapstone, bone, ivory, and antler — animals (polar bears, walrus, seals), human figures, and transformation figures showing beings between forms. Inuit sculpture is widely known outside the Arctic but is often misunderstood as purely commercial; the tradition has deep roots in material culture and spiritual practice.
- **Yup'ik masks** — among the most formally inventive objects in any tradition. Ceremonial masks made for specific dances and purposes; many were intentionally destroyed after use. Collected aggressively by European and American museums in the late 19th/early 20th century.
- **Cape Dorset / Kinngait Studios** (founded 1959, Baffin Island) — one of the most significant printmaking programs in the world. Artists like Kenojuak Ashevak and Pitseolak Ashoona created a distinctive graphic vocabulary. Still active and internationally recognized.
- **Clothing as art** — parkas, gut-skin garments, and footwear represent extraordinary technical and aesthetic achievement. Not "craft"; often the primary medium for specific makers.

## 2. Northwest Coast

**Peoples:** Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian, Gitxsan, Nisga'a, Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, Coast Salish, and others from Southeast Alaska to Northern California.

**Key traditions:**
- **Formline design** — the defining visual system of Northwest Coast art. A grammar of connected, flowing forms (primary formline, secondary U-forms and S-forms, tertiary design elements) used to represent crests and beings on any surface: totem poles, bentwood boxes, canoes, woven blankets, drums, jewelry. Has rules — not decorative free-form.
- **Totem poles** — carved cedar poles encoding clan history, crest figures, and narrative. Not "idols" or religious objects in the way often mischaracterized; they are records, markers, and statements of identity and history.
- **Bentwood boxes** — made from a single plank of cedar steamed and bent at four corners; used for storage, cooking, and ceremonial purposes. The engineering and the design painted on the surface are equally sophisticated.
- **Button blankets** — trade-era innovation: wool blankets with appliquéd felt designs and pearl buttons outlining clan crests. A living and evolving tradition.
- **Coast Salish weaving** — distinctive from formline traditions of the northern coast; Salish textiles used mountain goat wool and dog wool to create geometric blankets of considerable technical complexity.
- **Potlatch regalia** — the potlatch (a ceremony of gift-giving, feasting, and status transfer, banned by Canada 1885–1951) was inseparable from art production. Masks, rattles, coppers, and regalia were made for potlatches.

**Key artists:** Bill Reid (Haida, 1920–1998) — major figure who revived and reinterpreted Haida forms for a contemporary audience; his work is on the Canadian $20 bill. Robert Davidson (Haida) — widely considered the preeminent living Northwest Coast artist. Susan Point (Musqueam, Coast Salish).

## 3. Plateau

**Peoples:** Nez Perce, Yakama, Umatilla, Cayuse, Spokane, Flathead/Salish, Colville, and others in the Columbia Plateau region.

**Key traditions:**
- **Cornhusk bags** — flat twined bags made from cornhusks and hemp, featuring geometric designs in false embroidery. Among the most technically sophisticated textiles in North America. Still made.
- **Beadwork** — intricate floral and geometric beadwork on garments, bags, and horse gear. Plateau beadwork often features large floral designs on a white background.
- **Horse culture art** — after horses arrived (early 18th century), elaborate horse gear (martingales, saddle bags, cruppers) became a major art form.

## 4. Plains

**Peoples:** Lakota/Sioux, Cheyenne, Arapaho, Crow/Apsáalooke, Blackfoot/Niitsitapi, Comanche, Kiowa, and many others across the Great Plains.

**Key traditions:**
- **Ledger art** — one of the most significant artistic developments of the post-contact era. Plains warriors and artists adapted the pictographic tradition of hide painting to paper — ledger books, paper, and later canvas. Originally depicting battle exploits and personal history; after confinement to reservations, ledger art became a record of loss, memory, and resistance. Some of the most powerful visual documents of the 19th century. Key artists: Howling Wolf (Cheyenne), Making Medicine, Cohoe. The tradition continues with contemporary ledger artists like Virgil Ortiz and many others.
- **Hide painting** — buffalo robes and tipi covers painted with battle scenes, geometric designs (women's tradition), and personal history. Women and men painted distinct styles.
- **Beadwork** — after trade beads became available, Plains beadwork achieved extraordinary density and precision. Lakota beadwork is particularly known for fully beaded garments and cradle boards. Crow beadwork uses a distinctive palette (pink, blue, yellow, green) and style.
- **Quillwork** — older than beadwork; porcupine quills dyed and worked into geometric patterns on hide, bark, and fabric. A specialized and honored tradition particularly associated with Lakota and Cheyenne women's societies.
- **War shirts, dresses, and regalia** — fully beaded or quilled garments as records of identity, achievement, and relationship.

## 5. Southwest

**Peoples:** Diné (Navajo), Pueblo peoples (Hopi, Zuni, Acoma, Laguna, Taos, Santa Clara, San Ildefonso, and many others), Apache, Tohono O'odham, and others.

**Key traditions:**
- **Navajo weaving** — among the most recognized Indigenous art forms. Navajo weavers shifted from blanket to rug production in the late 19th century in response to trader demand. Major regional styles: Ganado (red, black, white, grey geometric), Two Grey Hills (natural wool colors, intricate geometry), Teec Nos Pos (outlined geometric), Chinle (banded), and others. Each trader-era style has a geographic origin. Contemporary Navajo weavers work across the full spectrum from traditional to experimental. The loom, the designs, and the wool source all carry cultural weight.
- **Pueblo pottery** — each Pueblo has its own clay, temper, forms, and design vocabulary. San Ildefonso (and Maria Martinez in particular) developed the matte-on-black style now widely associated with Pueblo pottery; this was an early 20th century innovation, not ancient. Acoma is known for thin-walled white pottery with fine-line geometric decoration. Hopi for complex polychrome. Santa Clara for carved black-on-black. Pottery is not merely decorative — clay, designs, and firing are relational practices.
- **Silversmithing** — Diné (Navajo) and Zuni smiths developed distinctive silver and turquoise traditions beginning in the mid-19th century. Squash blossom necklaces, concho belts, cuff bracelets. Turquoise is not merely decorative; its sources (mines in the Southwest) have specific cultural and spiritual associations.
- **Hopi kachina carving (tithu)** — figures representing katsina spirits, carved from cottonwood root. Originally made as teaching objects for children; now also collected as art. The commercialization and reproduction of kachina figures by non-Native makers is a persistent appropriation issue.
- **Sand painting (dry painting)** — Navajo (and some Pueblo) ceremonial practice involving colored sand, pollen, and pigments arranged in complex cosmological imagery on the ground for healing ceremonies. Traditionally destroyed at the end of the ceremony. Commercial "sand paintings" for the art market are adaptations, not the ceremony itself.

## 6. California and Great Basin

**Peoples:** Pomo, Yurok, Karuk, Hupa, Washoe, Paiute, Shoshone, Chumash, and hundreds of others — California alone had extraordinary linguistic and cultural diversity before contact.

**Key traditions:**
- **Basketry** — California produced some of the most technically refined basketry in the world. Pomo gift baskets (covered in shells, feathers, and beads) are extraordinary objects. Yurok and Karuk baskets are known for fine twining and design. Washoe weaver Dat So La Lee (Louisa Keyser) was among the first Native artists promoted under her own name to collectors in the early 20th century.
- **Shell work** — abalone, clam disk beads, and Olivella shells used for currency, adornment, and ceremonial objects throughout California.
- **Rock art** — extensive petroglyph and pictograph traditions across California and the Great Basin; Chumash cave paintings are particularly significant.

## 7. Woodlands (Northeast and Great Lakes)

**Peoples:** Anishinaabe (Ojibwe/Chippewa, Ottawa, Potawatomi), Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora), Lenape, Menominee, Ho-Chunk, and many others.

**Key traditions:**
- **Anishinaabe beadwork and floral design** — distinctive flowing floral patterns in beadwork, reflecting both older quillwork traditions and post-contact influences. The Woodland floral tradition is one of the most recognizable aesthetic vocabularies in North American Indigenous art.
- **Woodland painting / Anishinaabe legend painting** — Norval Morrisseau (Anishinaabe, 1932–2007) developed a painting style drawing on birchbark scrolls and the Midewiwin ceremonial tradition that became the Woodland school or Legend painting movement. Bold outlines, X-ray-style figures, flowing forms. He is sometimes called the "Picasso of the North" — a characterization that centers Western art history in a way worth noting; his work is significant on its own terms.
- **Haudenosaunee beadwork** — Iroquois raised beadwork (Victorian-influenced but Indigenous in conception) on velvet; also false embroidery, and moosehair embroidery in earlier periods.
- **Wampum belts** — belts of white and purple shell beads encoding treaty agreements, historical events, and diplomatic relationships. Not mere decoration; wampum belts are records with legal and political standing. Significant repatriation efforts ongoing.
- **Quillwork** — both Anishinaabe and Haudenosaunee traditions; birchbark boxes with quilled designs were a major early trade item and remain a practiced art form.
- **Gustoweh** (Haudenosaunee feathered headgear) and other regalia — nation-specific forms that encode identity and standing.

## 8. Southeast

**Peoples:** Cherokee, Creek/Mvskoke, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Seminole, Catawba, and others — many forcibly removed to Oklahoma in the 1830s (Trail of Tears).

**Key traditions:**
- **Mississippian tradition** — the mound-building cultures of the Southeast (c. 800–1600 CE) produced sophisticated copper repoussé work, shell gorgets with complex imagery, and ceramic traditions that fed later Southeastern art.
- **Cherokee basketry** — river cane double-weave baskets with complex geometric designs; river cane itself is an endangered plant, making the tradition doubly fragile.
- **Creek/Mvskoke pottery** — distinctive stamped pottery continuing pre-contact traditions.
- **Seminole patchwork** — a relatively recent tradition (post-1900, made possible by sewing machines) producing strips of interlocking geometric patchwork in vivid colors, now central to Seminole and Miccosukee identity.
- **Beadwork and regalia** — each nation has distinct traditions; Choctaw beadwork and appliqué work are particularly notable.
