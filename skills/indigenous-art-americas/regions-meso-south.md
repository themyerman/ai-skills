## 1. Mesoamerica

The term covers roughly modern Mexico and Central America, and encompasses extraordinary cultural diversity and continuity from the Olmec through contemporary Maya and Nahua communities.

### Olmec (c. 1500–400 BCE)
The earliest complex culture in Mesoamerica, centered on the Gulf Coast. Known for:
- **Colossal heads** — monumental basalt portraits, likely of rulers, up to 3 meters tall and 8 tons.
- **Jade work** — the Olmec established jade (jadeite) as the prestige material of Mesoamerica; this persisted through the Maya and Aztec periods.
- Early iconography of were-jaguar figures and rain deity imagery that influenced later cultures.

### Teotihuacan (c. 100 BCE–600 CE)
A city of perhaps 125,000 at its height, near modern Mexico City. Significant for:
- Extensive **mural painting** covering temple and residential walls — sophisticated polychrome scenes of deities, rituals, and daily life.
- **Thin Orange ware** ceramics traded across Mesoamerica.
- Architectural sculpture including the Avenue of the Dead and Pyramid of the Sun.

### Maya (c. 2000 BCE – present)
One of the great art-producing civilizations of the ancient world, with a continuous contemporary presence of millions of Maya people in Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, and El Salvador.

**Classic period (250–900 CE) achievements:**
- **Murals** — Bonampak murals (c. 790 CE) are the most complete surviving Maya mural cycle; depict battle, ceremony, and sacrifice in extraordinary detail and naturalism.
- **Relief sculpture** — carved stelae recording royal histories in full-figure compositions with hieroglyphic texts. The ability to read Maya glyphs (decipherment largely completed by the late 20th century) has transformed understanding of these as historical records.
- **Ceramics** — polychrome vessels with mythological scenes and court life, often accompanied by hieroglyphic texts. Codex-style ceramics are a distinct high-prestige tradition.
- **Jade** — jade was the most precious material in Maya culture; mosaic funeral masks, pendants, and offerings represent extraordinary lapidary work.
- **Architecture as art** — corbelled vaults, elaborate roof combs, mask facades; Palenque, Tikal, Copán, Uxmal each have distinct architectural vocabularies.

**Codices** — screenfold bark-paper books with astronomical, ritual, and divinatory content. Only four pre-contact Maya codices survive (Dresden, Madrid, Paris, and Grolier/Mexico). The Dresden Codex contains the most accurate Venus tables produced anywhere in the pre-telescopic world.

**Contemporary Maya art:** Living Maya artists and weavers continue pre-contact traditions alongside contemporary practice. Maya textile weaving (backstrap loom, huipiles with community-specific designs) remains a central cultural practice, with each community having distinct color palettes and patterns that encode identity and cosmology.

### Zapotec and Mixtec
Civilizations of Oaxaca, Mexico.
- **Zapotec** built Monte Albán (500 BCE onward), produced elaborate **funerary urns** in ceramic with deity figures, and developed one of the earliest writing systems in the Americas.
- **Mixtec** are known for **screenfold codices** — the Mixtec codices (Nuttall, Bodley, Vindobonensis, and others) are among the oldest surviving pre-contact books in the Americas, recording genealogical and historical narratives in a highly developed pictographic system.
- Mixtec **metalwork** (gold and silver) and **turquoise mosaic** work were among the finest in Mesoamerica; Aztec rulers collected Mixtec craftspeople.

### Mexica / Aztec (c. 1300–1521 CE)
The Mexica/Aztec empire at its peak was the dominant power in central Mexico and a major consumer and redistributor of art from across Mesoamerica.
- **Featherwork** — amanteca featherworkers produced headdresses, shields, and garments of extraordinary intricacy using iridescent feathers (quetzal, cotinga, hummingbird). The so-called "Moctezuma's headdress" in Vienna is one of the most contested repatriation cases in the world.
- **Turquoise mosaic** — skulls, masks, and shields encrusted with turquoise, shell, and pyrite.
- **Stone sculpture** — the Calendar Stone (often misnamed the "Aztec calendar") is actually a solar monument; the Coatlicue statue is one of the most powerful sculptural achievements of any civilization.
- **Codices** — both pre-contact and colonial-period codices survive, including the Codex Mendoza (documenting tribute and history for Spanish administrators).

## 2. Caribbean

### Taíno
The Taíno people occupied the Greater Antilles (Cuba, Hispaniola, Puerto Rico, Jamaica) and the Bahamas at the time of European contact. Rapid depopulation due to disease, enslavement, and violence by the early 16th century severely disrupted their culture, though Taíno ancestry and cultural revival movements are significant today.

**Key art forms:**
- **Zemis** — three-pointed stone or shell objects embodying ancestral and supernatural powers. Zemis were the focus of ritual and spiritual life and represent some of the most formally inventive objects of Caribbean Indigenous culture.
- **Duhos** — ceremonial seats/thrones carved in wood or stone, sometimes inlaid with gold; used by caciques (leaders) during ceremonial occasions.
- **Petroglyphs** — widespread across the Caribbean islands; ball court petroglyphs at Caguana (Puerto Rico) are particularly significant.
- **Gold work** — guanín, a gold-copper-silver alloy, was more valued than pure gold for its color and smell; used in pendants and ornaments.

## 3. Andes

The Andean region — covering modern Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and parts of Chile, Colombia, and Argentina — produced one of the world's most technically sophisticated textile traditions, and the Inca empire was the largest in the pre-contact Americas.

### Textiles as primary medium
In the Andes, textiles were the prestige medium — more valued than gold or silver. Understanding this is essential to understanding Andean art.

- **Tocapu** — geometric woven emblems on Inca garments encoding rank, identity, and cosmological information. The grammar of tocapu is not fully decoded.
- **Cumbi cloth** — the finest Inca weave (up to 600 threads per inch), made from vicuña fiber, worn only by the Inca ruler and given as gifts of state. Among the most technically fine textiles ever produced.
- **Quipu** — knotted cord recording devices used across the Inca empire. Not art in a conventional sense, but complex information systems in fiber; their full content is still being decoded.
- **Wari and Tiwanaku textiles** (c. 600–1000 CE) — vivid tapestry-woven tunics with interlocking geometric figures, some of the most visually complex pre-Inca textiles.
- **Contemporary Andean weaving** — Quechua and Aymara weaving communities in Peru and Bolivia maintain living traditions, with community-specific designs, natural dyes, and backstrap looms.

### Ceramics
- **Moche** (c. 100–800 CE, northern Peru) — portrait vessels depicting individual faces with apparent specificity; narrative spout vessels depicting mythological scenes. Among the most sophisticated ceramics of the ancient world.
- **Nazca** (c. 100 BCE–800 CE, southern Peru) — polychrome ceramics and the Nazca Lines — geoglyphs covering hundreds of square kilometers visible from the air, depicting animals, plants, and geometric forms. Their purpose remains a subject of scholarship.
- **Marajó Island** (mouth of the Amazon, Brazil) — large funerary urns and female figurines in polychrome with incised geometric decoration; among the most visually striking ceramics in South America.

### Metalwork
- **Chimú** (c. 900–1470 CE, northern Peru) — extraordinary gold and silver work; the largest surviving collection of pre-Inca metalwork.
- **Inca** — gold and silver objects represented the sun and moon respectively; much was melted down by Spanish colonizers. Surviving pieces show sophisticated hammering, soldering, and casting.
- **Muisca** (Colombia) — the legend of El Dorado derives partly from Muisca gold ritual: gilded figures cast by lost-wax method, gold offerings thrown into sacred lakes.

## 4. Amazon Basin

The Amazon is often underrepresented in discussions of pre-contact art because organic materials (feathers, bark cloth, body paint) do not survive archaeologically and because the region lacked monumental architecture. This is a failure of the archive, not of the cultures.

**Key traditions:**
- **Body painting** — in many Amazonian cultures, body painting is the primary art form. Kayapó, Xingu peoples, and others use genipap (black) and annatto (red) pigments to cover the body with intricate geometric designs encoding social status, ritual role, and cosmological meaning. Body painting is not permanent; it is renewed, seasonal, relational. To treat it as lesser because it doesn't survive in museums is to apply inappropriate criteria.
- **Featherwork** — Kayapó, Tupinambá, and other Amazonian peoples produced headdresses, capes, and ornaments of extraordinary complexity. Tupinambá feather mantles in European collections (from early contact) are among the most significant surviving objects of Amazonian origin.
- **Ceramics** — the Amazon had sophisticated ceramic traditions; Marajó Island (see Andes section) is the best-known.
- **Shipibo-Konibo** (Peru) — a living tradition of geometric pattern-making (kené) appearing on pottery, textiles, and body painting, associated with ayahuasca visionary experience. Contemporary Shipibo-Konibo artists have brought kené to large-scale contemporary art contexts.

## 5. Southern Cone

### Mapuche (Chile and Argentina)
One of the few peoples to successfully resist Inca and then Spanish conquest for centuries (the Arauco War lasted from 1536 to 1818). Art as cultural assertion is central to Mapuche identity.

- **Silverwork (Retrafe)** — Mapuche silver jewelry (trapelacucha breast ornaments, earrings, headpieces) is among the most culturally significant jewelry traditions in the Americas. The silversmith tradition (retrafe) carries spiritual and social weight. Silver was adopted from Spanish sources but transformed into a distinctly Mapuche visual and cultural vocabulary. Contemporary Mapuche artists continue and reinterpret these forms.
- **Textiles** — Mapuche women's weaving (backstrap and frame loom) with geometric patterns and natural dyes; designs encode cosmological knowledge and identity.

### Cave art
- **Cueva de las Manos** (Patagonia, Argentina, c. 9,000–1,000 BCE) — one of the oldest and most extensive rock art sites in the Americas. Hand stencils (hundreds of negative hands) alongside hunting scenes and geometric designs. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

### Selk'nam (Tierra del Fuego)
- **Body painting** — elaborate geometric body painting for the Hain initiation ceremony. Photographs by Martin Gusinde (1919–1924) are the primary visual record; the Selk'nam were nearly exterminated by settlers in the late 19th century. Contemporary Selk'nam descendants are engaged in cultural revival.
