## Woodland school / Pictographic painting

**Where:** Great Lakes, Ontario, Manitoba — centered on Anishinaabe territory  
**When:** 1960s–present

Norval Morrisseau (see [artists.md](artists.md)) developed Woodland painting in the early 1960s drawing on Anishinaabe birchbark scroll imagery and Midewiwin ceremony. The visual vocabulary is distinctive: bold black outlines, x-ray figures revealing internal organs and spiritual energy, forms that flow into and through each other, vivid primary and secondary colors. It was controversial within his community for making sacred imagery visible outside ceremony — and transformative for Indigenous art broadly because it demonstrated that a living ceremonial tradition could anchor a fully contemporary painting practice.

### Professional Native Indian Artists Incorporation ("Indian Group of Seven")

In 1973, seven artists formally organized as the Professional Native Indian Artists Incorporation, sometimes called the "Indian Group of Seven" (a deliberate echo/challenge to the Canadian Group of Seven landscape painters who had romanticized Indigenous land while ignoring Indigenous people):

- **Norval Morrisseau** (Anishinaabe) — founder of the movement; see [artists.md](artists.md)
- **Daphne Odjig** (Potawatomi/Odawa, 1919–2016) — painter and printmaker; her work ranges from Woodland-inflected figuration to near-abstraction; she was also a central organizer and mentor to other artists in the group and beyond
- **Jackson Beardy** (Oji-Cree, 1944–1984) — paintings with a strong spiritual and cosmological dimension; influenced by both Morrisseau and his own Cree ceremonial background
- **Eddy Cobiness** (Ojibwe, 1933–1996) — fluid, lyrical paintings; wildlife and landscape through an Anishinaabe lens
- **Alex Janvier** (Dene/Saulteaux, b. 1935) — distinctive dot-and-line style; his work ranges from abstraction to figurative; one of the longest active careers in the group; a residential school survivor whose work carries that history without being defined by it
- **Carl Ray** (Cree, 1943–1978) — collaborative work with Morrisseau; bold spiritual imagery; died young
- **Joseph Sanchez** (Anishinaabe) — painter; less documented than others in the group

The group's significance was collective as much as individual: they organized, exhibited together, and made the argument that contemporary Indigenous artists working from their own traditions deserved the same institutional recognition as other Canadian artists. That argument had to be made explicitly at the time — and it changed what was possible.

**Legacy:** The Woodland school remains a living tradition. Many subsequent artists work in or in dialogue with it. Blake Debassige, Roy Thomas, and others extended the vocabulary. The forgery crisis around Morrisseau's work (thousands of fakes entered the market) is also a legacy — it reflects both the value the work achieved and the ongoing vulnerability of Indigenous artists and their estates to market exploitation.

## Northwest Coast formline revival and contemporary practice

**Where:** Pacific Northwest coast — from northern California through British Columbia to southeast Alaska  
**When:** Living tradition; contemporary revival codified from 1965 onward

Northwest Coast Indigenous art has one of the most codified and technically demanding visual systems in the world. The formline design system — which governs how ovoid forms, U-forms, and S-forms combine to create figures on flat and three-dimensional surfaces — was practiced continuously through the colonial period, though its transmission was severely disrupted. Bill Holm, a non-Indigenous art historian, published *Northwest Coast Indian Art: An Analysis of Form* in 1965, which for the first time provided a systematic written description of the rules. This was both useful (it helped artists and students learn the system) and controversial (an outsider codifying an Indigenous tradition, in a way that gave his analytical vocabulary authority).

### The tradition

**What formline is:** A continuous curvilinear design system in which the primary form (typically black) is always present and always flows; secondary forms (typically red) fill the spaces; tertiary forms (blue-green) fill remaining spaces. The system is used to depict clan crests, supernatural beings, and historical narratives on totem poles, house fronts, boxes, bowls, regalia, and canoes. The rules are real — a trained eye can identify violations — but within them there is enormous room for individual style.

**Media:** Red cedar carving (totem poles, boxes, masks, paddles, canoes), argillite (Haida carved stone), silver and gold jewelry, woven Chilkat and Ravenstail robes, printmaking, painting on flat surfaces.

**Crests and clan ownership:** Northwest Coast art is not decorative in a Western sense. The images are owned — by clans, families, and lineages. Displaying a crest you don't own is a serious protocol violation. Understanding this is essential to understanding why this work cannot be separated from governance and social structure.

### Key contemporary artists

**Bill Reid** (Haida, 1920–1998) — probably the most internationally recognized Northwest Coast artist of the 20th century. He came to the tradition as an adult (he was raised largely outside Haida culture) and worked intensively to master and then extend it. His large bronze *The Spirit of Haida Gwaii* (the "Jade Canoe") is installed at the Vancouver International Airport and at the Canadian Embassy in Washington. His jewelry work in gold and silver brought Haida formline to a new international market. His relationship to tradition and identity is itself a complex story — he was open about having learned the system as an adult outsider to his own community.

**Robert Davidson** (Haida, b. 1946) — grandson of master carver Charles Edenshaw; one of the most technically accomplished and influential living Northwest Coast artists. Erected the first totem pole in Haida Gwaii in nearly a century (1969). His work spans carving, jewelry, printmaking, and painting; he has also been a central figure in the revitalization of Haida cultural practices including the potlatch.

**Susan Point** (Musqueam/Coast Salish, b. 1952) — largely responsible for the contemporary revival and recognition of Coast Salish design as distinct from the Northern formline tradition. Coast Salish visual art had been overshadowed by Haida and Kwakwaka'wakw work in the market; Point's sustained practice of printmaking, carving, and public art established it as a major tradition with its own vocabulary.

**Dempsey Bob** (Tahltan-Tlingit, b. 1948) — master carver known for masks and poles; his work has a psychological intensity and roughness that distinguishes it from more polished formline work.

**Lawrence Paul Yuxweluptun** (Syilx/Nlaka'pamux, b. 1957) — painter who explicitly combines Northwest Coast formal elements with surrealism and political critique. His large canvases use formline figures in post-apocalyptic or dystopian landscapes, directly engaging colonialism, land rights, and environmental destruction. One of the most politically direct practitioners working with Northwest Coast visual vocabulary.

## Kinngait (Cape Dorset) printmaking

**Where:** Kinngait, Nunavut (formerly Cape Dorset), Baffin Island  
**When:** 1959–present

The West Baffin Eskimo Co-operative (now Kinngait Studios) was established in 1959 by James Houston, who recognized that Inuit artists were producing extraordinary drawings and worked to develop printmaking as a medium that could bring those drawings to market. The annual Kinngait print collection — released each year since 1959 — became one of the most internationally collected bodies of Indigenous art in the world.

The significance of Kinngait is difficult to overstate. It demonstrated that a community-controlled studio program could achieve international recognition while keeping creative control with the artists. It created sustainable income for Inuit artists. And it produced a body of work — in stonecut, stencil, and later lithography and etching — that is visually extraordinary.

### Key artists

**Kenojuak Ashevak** (1927–2013) — the most internationally recognized Kinngait artist; her image *The Enchanted Owl* (1960) became iconic. She worked in drawing and printmaking for over five decades; her sinuous, symmetrical animal and human figures are immediately recognizable.

**Pitseolak Ashoona** (c. 1904–1983) — began drawing in her late 50s; produced thousands of drawings of Inuit traditional life from memory. Her autobiography *Pitseolak: Pictures Out of My Life* is a primary document of both the art and the life.

**Pitaloosie Saila** (1942–2021) — prints and drawings; work often centered on women's experience and the natural world; one of the most prolific Kinngait artists of her generation.

**Kananginak Pootoogook** (1935–2010) — known for detailed drawings of Arctic wildlife and traditional Inuit tools and practices; a central figure in the co-operative.

**Annie Pootoogook** (1969–2016) — granddaughter of Pitseolak Ashoona; her work was a significant departure from the wildlife and traditional-life imagery associated with Kinngait, depicting contemporary Inuit domestic life — television sets, abuse, boredom, contemporary clothing — with the same direct draftsmanship. Won the Sobey Art Award in 2006. Her work forced a reckoning with the art world's preference for "traditional" Inuit imagery over contemporary Inuit experience. She died in Ottawa under circumstances that remain contested; her death became part of the MMIWG conversation.

### What to know in conversation
Kinngait work is sometimes treated as "craft" or "folk art" rather than fine art — a categorization the work and its artists actively resist. Annie Pootoogook's career is particularly useful here: her deliberate break from expected Inuit imagery to depict contemporary life was partly a statement about who gets to decide what counts as authentic Indigenous art.

## Indigenous photography and archival reclamation

**Where:** North America primarily  
**When:** 1980s–present

Edward Curtis photographed thousands of Indigenous people across North America between 1900 and 1930, staging many images to present an "authentic" and "vanishing" Native America — often removing contemporary objects from the frame, providing subjects with props or costumes they didn't use, and constructing images of pre-contact life that were already historical fiction. His photographs became the dominant visual representation of Indigenous people for most of the 20th century.

A significant movement of Indigenous photographers has emerged since the 1980s that engages directly with this legacy and with photographic representation more broadly — both by making new images and by returning to archives and reframing what's there.

### Key artists and approaches

**Wendy Red Star** (Crow/Apsáalooke, b. 1981) — see [artists.md](artists.md). Her work with 19th-century photographs of Crow delegates restores names, context, and Indigenous perspective to images made for an extractive ethnographic gaze.

**Hulleah Tsinhnahjinnie** (Seminole/Muscogee/Diné, b. 1954) — photographer and curator. Her series *Portraits Against Amnesia* and *Without Boundaries* directly engage with photography's history as a tool for documenting and "preserving" Indigenous peoples (i.e., treating them as already gone). Her work and writing have been central to theorizing Indigenous photography as a distinct practice.

**Zig Jackson** (Mandan/Hidatsa/Arikara) — his "Indian Photographing Tourist Photographing Indian" series turns the camera around: he photographs tourists photographing him, making visible the dynamic of the Indigenous person as spectacle and the camera as instrument of that dynamic.

**Will Wilson** (Diné/Navajo, b. 1969) — his *Critical Indigenous Photographic Exchange* project uses a large-format wet plate collodion process — the same technology as 19th-century ethnographic photography — to make new portraits of Indigenous people on their own terms. The technical choice is deliberate: reclaiming the tool used to construct the "vanishing Indian" image.

**Pamela Shields Carroll** — work with archival photographs of Indigenous people; contextualization and community return of images.

### The archive question
Many historical photographs of Indigenous people are held in institutional archives — the Smithsonian, the Library of Congress, university collections — where they remain under institutional copyright even though the subjects are Indigenous people who were photographed without meaningful consent and whose descendants have no control over how the images are used. This is an active rights and repatriation question, related to but distinct from the physical object repatriation issues covered by NAGPRA.

## Native American modernism and the IAIA generation

**Where:** Santa Fe, New Mexico; broader US  
**When:** 1930s–present; IAIA founded 1962

The trajectory from institutional "flat style" to self-determined modernism is one of the defining stories of 20th-century Native American art.

Dorothy Dunn's Studio School at the Santa Fe Indian School (1932–1937) trained a generation of artists in a specific "traditional" style — flat, decorative, featuring recognizable Native subjects — that Dunn and her patrons considered authentically Indigenous. The style was actually a construction: many of the conventions were imposed by non-Indigenous taste-makers rather than derived from actual traditions. Artists were actively discouraged from other approaches.

Oscar Howe's 1958 letter to the Philbrook Museum (see [artists.md](artists.md)) marked the public break. Fritz Scholder's arrival at IAIA as a teacher in 1964 accelerated it. IAIA — the Institute of American Indian Arts, founded in Santa Fe in 1962 — was explicitly designed to support a broader range of contemporary practice. Its alumni include T.C. Cannon, Jaune Quick-to-See Smith, Earl Biss, Kevin Red Star, Linda Lomahaftewa, and dozens of others.

The IAIA generation's achievement was establishing that Indigenous artists could work in the full register of contemporary Western art — abstraction, expressionism, conceptualism, pop — without surrendering Indigenous identity or subject matter. This sounds obvious now; it was not obvious in 1964, and it required sustained argument and institutional support.

## Land-based and site-specific art

**Where:** North America  
**When:** 1970s–present; intensified post-Standing Rock (2016)

A significant strand of contemporary Indigenous art is explicitly connected to specific land — not landscape painting in a Western romantic tradition, but art that enacts relationship to place, that makes visible what the land holds, or that intervenes in ongoing land and sovereignty conflicts.

**Bently Spang** (Northern Cheyenne) — installation work using traditional materials (teepee poles, canvas) in industrial contexts; "War Shirt" series.

**Nora Naranjo-Morse** (Santa Clara Pueblo, b. 1953) — ceramics and large outdoor installation; *Always Becoming* at the National Museum of the American Indian; work rooted in Tewa relationship to clay and earth.

**Alan Michelson** (Mohawk, b. 1953) — video and installation work engaging with colonial history of specific places, particularly in New York and the Northeast.

**Postcommodity** — see [indigenous-futurism.md](indigenous-futurism.md); *Repellent Fence* spanning the US/Mexico border is a major land-based work.

**Cannupa Hanska Luger** — mirror shields; ongoing large-scale community projects tied to specific land and political moments.

The **Land Back movement** (the political demand for the return of Indigenous lands) has become explicitly connected to visual art and curatorial practice since roughly 2018. Many Indigenous artists now situate their work directly within Land Back as a political framework.

## Two-Spirit and LGBTQ+ Indigenous art

Many Indigenous nations have long recognized gender identities and roles beyond the European binary — often gathered under the contemporary umbrella term "Two-Spirit," though specific terms and meanings vary by nation. Colonial law, Christianity, and residential schools actively suppressed these traditions. A significant body of contemporary Indigenous art engages with Two-Spirit identity, queer Indigenous experience, and the reclamation of pre-colonial gender frameworks.

**Kent Monkman** (Cree) — his Miss Chief Eagle Testickle persona explicitly engages with Two-Spirit identity and with the history of colonial suppression of queer and gender-nonconforming Indigenous people; see [artists.md](artists.md).

**Geo Neptune** (Passamaquoddy) — beadwork artist and activist; work centers Two-Spirit identity and traditional Passamaquoddy design.

**Thirza Cuthand** (Plains Cree/Scottish, b. 1978) — video artist; work deals with mental health, queerness, and Indigenous identity with humor and directness.

This is an area of active artistic production and critical development — relatively recent in terms of mainstream art world visibility but rooted in traditions much older than that visibility.
