# Design feedback vocabulary

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

A shared language for describing and critiquing visual work. The goal is to move from "something feels off" to a diagnosis with a direction.

---

## 1. Giving useful feedback

**Symptom → diagnosis → suggestion.** Name what you see, then why it's happening, then one possible direction:

> "The image feels busy [symptom] because four elements are competing at equal visual weight [diagnosis]. Try darkening three of them so one clearly dominates [suggestion]."

**Separate structural observations from personal preference.** "I don't like purple" is different from "the purple mid-tones are reading as grey because they're close in value to the background." The second is actionable.

**Describe what the eye does**, not just what you think. "The eye keeps getting pulled to the bottom-left corner before it's ready" is more useful than "the bottom-left is distracting."

## 2. Composition vocabulary

**Visual weight** — how dominant an element feels. Heavier elements: dark, large, saturated, textured, isolated, or at the edge of the frame. Weight creates balance or tension.

**Balance** — symmetrical (mirror image, stable, formal) vs. asymmetrical (different elements at equal weight, more dynamic). Neither is better; both are valid. Tension is not the same as imbalance.

**Focal point** — where the eye lands first. Usually the highest contrast area, the most isolated element, or the most detailed region. A strong composition has a clear primary focal point and can have one or two secondary ones — not five competing equals.

**Visual flow / eye movement** — how the eye travels through the image. Leading lines, gaze direction, value gradients, and repetition all guide movement. Ask: where does the eye enter, where does it go, where does it get stuck or fall off the edge?

**Figure / ground** — the relationship between subject and background. When they're too similar in value or texture they merge and fight. Sometimes intentional; usually not.

**Negative space** — the empty areas around and between subjects. Negative space is active: it gives the composition room to breathe, creates shape, and controls pace. "Cramped" usually means not enough negative space. "Floating" often means negative space is unintentional and equal on all sides.

**Tension** — when elements push against each other or against the frame edge. Tension creates energy and can be intentional and powerful.

**Cropping** — what you leave out matters as much as what you include. Tight crops create intimacy; loose crops create context. Cropping into a subject (cutting off limbs or objects at the frame edge) draws the eye to the cut.

## 3. Color vocabulary

**Hue** — the color family: red, blue, gold, teal. The name of the color.

**Saturation / chroma** — how pure and vivid vs. dull and grey. Saturated colors advance and demand attention. Desaturated colors recede. A single saturated color in a desaturated field pulls the eye powerfully.

**Value / luminosity** — how light or dark. Value does the structural heavy lifting in composition. Check your work in greyscale: if the composition doesn't read, value structure is the problem, not color.

**Temperature** — warm (reds, oranges, yellows) vs. cool (blues, purples, greens). Warm colors tend to advance; cool colors recede. Warm light with cool shadows (or vice versa) creates depth. When everything is the same temperature, the image feels flat.

**Dominance** — one hue or temperature should dominate. Equal proportions of competing colors fight each other. A 70/20/10 split (dominant / secondary / accent) is a useful starting point.

**Accent** — a small amount of a contrasting or complementary color that draws the eye. Effective when used sparingly. When overused it becomes a competing color.

**Color harmonies:**

| Harmony | Relationship | Character |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Complementary | Opposite on the wheel | High contrast, vibrant, can fight if equal |
| Analogous | Neighbors on the wheel | Cohesive, calm, can feel flat without a value contrast |
| Triadic | Three equidistant | Vibrant, balanced, harder to pull off |
| Split-complementary | One color + two adjacent to its complement | High contrast without the clash of straight complementary |
| Monochromatic | One hue, varying value and saturation | Unified, sophisticated, needs strong value structure |

## 4. Typography vocabulary

**Hierarchy** — size, weight, and spacing establish reading order. If everything is bold, nothing is. A reader should know what to read first, second, and third without thinking about it.

**Contrast** — two typefaces in the same layout should differ enough to serve clearly different roles. A high-contrast pairing (serif heading + sans-serif body, or heavy weight + light weight) reads as intentional. Two similar fonts read as an accident.

**Leading** — line spacing. Too tight: claustrophobic, hard to read. Too loose: the lines feel disconnected. Body text typically reads well at 1.4–1.6× the font size.

**Tracking** — letter spacing across a word or block of text. Tight tracking increases density and tension. Loose tracking (especially in uppercase labels) creates air and formality.

**Texture** — a block of type has texture: the overall grey tone created by letterforms and spacing. Two blocks of type at different weights or sizes create visual contrast through texture alone.

## 5. Common problems and their names

**No focal point / "busy"** — too many elements at equal visual weight. Nothing clearly dominates. Fix: reduce the value, saturation, or size of secondary elements.

**Muddy colors** — low-saturation mid-values competing without clear light/dark contrast. Often happens when too many colors are mixed or when the value range is too narrow.

**Floating** — subject has no visual anchor; usually equal negative space on all sides with no shadow, ground plane, or value shift to root it.

**Fighting colors** — two highly saturated complementary colors in equal proportion. Each cancels the other's impact. Fix: reduce one to an accent role or desaturate one.

**Trapped negative space** — awkward empty shapes between elements that feel accidental rather than designed. Often happens when elements are positioned by content logic rather than visual logic.

**Type overwhelm** — too many weights, sizes, or typefaces with no clear hierarchy. Everything is shouting; nothing is heard.

**Tangents** — when an edge or line in the subject aligns exactly with the frame edge or another element's edge, creating an uncomfortable visual conflict. Usually fixed with a slight crop or reposition.

**Halation** — when two highly saturated colors of similar value sit next to each other, the edge between them vibrates. Can be intentional (op art) or distracting.

---

## 6. Checklist for reviewing a composition

- [ ] **Focal point** — is there one? Is it the right one? Is it clear?
- [ ] **Eye path** — where does the eye enter, travel, and exit or get stuck?
- [ ] **Value structure** — does the composition read in greyscale?
- [ ] **Color dominance** — is there a clear dominant hue and temperature?
- [ ] **Competing elements** — are any elements fighting at equal visual weight?
- [ ] **Negative space** — is it intentional or accidental?
- [ ] **Type hierarchy** — is reading order clear without effort?
- [ ] **Structural vs. preference** — which observations are about structure and which are personal?
