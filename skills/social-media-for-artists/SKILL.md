# social-media-for-artists

Social media is a tool for building an audience and a relationship with that audience — not a sales channel. The artists who do it well treat it as a place to share work and process, not a storefront. Sales follow trust; trust follows consistency and authenticity.

---

## What actually works

Before tactics: the underlying principle is that people follow artists because they want to see the work and understand the person making it. Show both. Work without personality is a portfolio. Personality without work is a personality account.

The posts that consistently perform well:
- **Process** — how you make things. Sketches, work in progress, decisions, mistakes.
- **Finished work with context** — not just the image; what it is, what it means, what you were thinking
- **Behind the scenes** — your workspace, your materials, your day
- **Personal perspective** — your relationship to the subject matter; what the work connects to in your life and heritage

The posts that don't work as well:
- "Buy my print" without anything else
- Generic motivational content that isn't connected to your work
- Reposting other artists without adding your voice

## Instagram

Instagram is still the primary platform for visual artists. The algorithm favors:
1. **Reels** — short video gets more reach than static posts
2. **Consistency** — accounts that post regularly get more distribution
3. **Saves and shares** — a saved post signals quality to the algorithm more than a like

### Posting frequency

3–5 times per week is sustainable and enough. Daily if you have the content. Once a week if that's all you can manage. Consistency matters more than frequency — don't set a pace you'll drop.

### Content mix

Across a week or two:
- 2–3 static posts or carousels — finished work, behind-the-scenes, personal
- 1–2 Reels — process, timelapse, narrated walkthrough of a piece
- Stories daily or near-daily — lower stakes, higher frequency, stays on top of mind

Stories don't need to be polished. A quick photo of what you're working on, a question, a poll about which version you prefer — they build a sense of ongoing presence.

### Captions

Write more than you think you should. Instagram rewards captions that keep people reading, and the algorithm measures "time on post." A caption that tells the story behind the work, shares what you were thinking, or asks a genuine question performs better than "New print available — link in bio."

### Hashtags

Hashtags still help with discovery, but less than they used to. Use 5–15 specific ones rather than 30 generic ones.

Good hashtag strategy:
- Niche-specific: `#indigenousdigitalart`, `#nativeart`, `#digitalart`
- Subject-specific: `#wolfart`, `#nativewildlife`, `#geometricart`
- Community: `#artistsoninstagram`, `#artofinstagram`
- Avoid: ultra-popular generic tags like `#art` — you'll be buried instantly

### Link in bio

Instagram gives you one link. Use a link aggregator (Linktree, Later's link page, a simple page on your own site) to point to your shop, Patreon, and current work — not just your homepage.

## TikTok

TikTok has an enormous discovery engine — content from new accounts can reach millions of people. The content style is different: faster, more casual, often with voiceover or trending audio.

What works for artists on TikTok:
- Timelapse process with popular audio
- "How I made this" with a voiceover explaining decisions
- Unexpected transformations (blank canvas → finished piece)
- Educational content: "here's why I chose this color palette"

TikTok audiences tend to be younger and more casual than Instagram. They engage high but convert to buyers less directly. Good for awareness; Instagram is better for nurturing buyers.

## Pinterest

Pinterest is a search engine for visual content, not a social network. People use it to find inspiration and save ideas — and pins can drive traffic for years, not hours.

For artists, Pinterest is worth setting up and maintaining with low effort:
- Pin your work with descriptive text (keywords people might search)
- Create boards that reflect your subject matter and aesthetic
- Pin work from your site, not just Instagram (it drives traffic back to you)

A good Pinterest presence works in the background while you're focused on Instagram.

## Captions that convert

Caption structure that works when you do want to sell:

1. Open with something about the work — the story, the subject, the decision
2. Middle: context that creates desire — the material, the edition size, why this piece is what it is
3. End: a clear invitation — "Available in the shop — link in bio" or "DMs open for commissions"

Don't open with the sell. Open with the work.

## Analytics: what to track

Check analytics monthly, not daily. Daily numbers create anxiety; monthly trends tell you something useful.

What matters:
- **Reach** — how many unique accounts saw your content
- **Profile visits** — are posts sending people to learn more about you?
- **Website clicks** — are they going to your shop?
- **Saves** — high saves = content people want to come back to

What to ignore:
- Follower count as a primary metric — 1,000 engaged followers who buy is better than 50,000 who don't
- Individual post performance — average over time, not individual outliers

## Converting followers to buyers

Social media builds awareness; the conversion usually happens elsewhere — your website, Fotomoto, Patreon. The path:

1. Follower sees consistent work and builds trust over time
2. A piece resonates enough to click through
3. Your shop is easy to navigate and clear about what's available
4. The purchase is simple

The friction points are usually: (a) the link in bio sends them somewhere unhelpful, (b) the shop is hard to navigate, (c) it's unclear what's for sale and at what price.

Email list beats social media for conversion. A follower who gives you their email address is 10x more likely to buy than one who only follows. Offer something (process PDF, desktop wallpaper, first look at new work) in exchange for joining your list.
