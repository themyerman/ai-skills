# Tom Myer — Visual Language

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

---

## Signature elements

These recurring elements define his visual identity. When they appear together, the work is unmistakably his.

**The sun circle** — A large, bold circle (yellow, orange, red, or gold) used as a compositional anchor. Present in nearly every piece. May sit behind a figure as a halo, above a landscape, or as the gravitational center of the composition. Not decorative — it carries spiritual and cosmological weight.

**Cross-hatched texture fields** — Backgrounds and mid-grounds built up with layered cross-hatching, scratch marks, and overlapping texture. Creates visual density and a hand-made quality. Often in warm reds, teals, or mixed color fields.

**Petroglyph marks** — Hand symbols, thunderbirds, geometric clan marks, medicine wheels, and other Indigenous iconographic elements woven through compositions. Used as structural elements, not decoration. Appear on animal bodies, in backgrounds, and floating in the composition.

**Bold silhouette work** — Strong dark silhouettes against hot color fields. Used for figures, animals, and landscape elements. Some of his most powerful work (MMIW, Ancestors Point the Way) is primarily silhouette.

**Palette** — Warm, saturated, high-contrast. Deep reds, burnt oranges, teals, turquoise, hot pinks, purple-violet, and gold. Dark near-blacks for silhouettes and outlines. Colors are emotional carriers, not naturalistic descriptions.

**Visible brushwork** — Hand-made quality is intentional. Marks are expressive and loose, never slick or over-rendered. The work reads as painted, not designed.

**Bold outlines** — Many figurative and animal pieces use heavy black outlines that define form and create graphic punch. See Coyote and Raven, Deer Woman.

## Compositional habits

- Figure or animal positioned left or center, sun positioned upper right or directly behind as halo
- Portrait orientation for figurative work, square or landscape for group compositions
- Dense foreground, activated background — no empty fields
- Silhouettes placed on a horizon line against a glowing sky
- Layered depth: foreground figure → mid-ground activity → background color field
- Animals often carry the same visual weight as human figures — non-hierarchical relationship between beings

## Physical practice: acrylics and pastels

Tom worked in acrylics and pastels before moving to digital. He does not currently have a dedicated studio space, which makes analog work impractical — not a creative choice to stop, a logistical one. The existing physical work represents a complete and significant body in its own right.

**What the physical work reveals about the digital:**

The digital vocabulary did not emerge from software — it was translated from physical practice. Specific origins:

- **The cross-hatched texture fields** come directly from pastel technique. Building tone and color through repeated directional strokes on textured paper is how pastels work. The digital cross-hatching is the same process reconstructed in Procreate.
- **The sun circle** appears in physical paintings as an impasto circle built up with thick circular brushstrokes — more dynamic and textured than the flat digital version. The physical origin is more energetic; the digital simplified it into a bold graphic element.
- **The bold outline style** (Coyote and Raven, Deer Woman) descends directly from the acrylic owl and wolf paintings, where heavy black outlines define form over non-naturalistic color.
- **Non-naturalistic animal color** — the bear rendered in orange/red/green/blue, the wolf in rainbow strokes — is a physical painting choice first, not a digital one.
- **The pastel face** (sketchbook) is a direct ancestor of Fractured Native: same multi-color face-building, same bold graphic nose line, same cross-hatching building color.
- **The atmospheric sky handling** in futurism pieces derives from physical acrylic sky work — loose, gestural, swirling.

**Two physical modes that merged in digital:**

In physical work Tom operated in two distinct modes:
1. **Expressionist** — gestural, emotional, built through accumulated marks (bear portrait, owl, tree/sun landscape, pastel face studies)
2. **Graphic** — bold line, flat color, structural (dragon head on raw canvas, abstract bicycle, pastel grid)

His digital practice is where these two modes fused into a single unified approach. That fusion is why the digital work looks like nothing else.

**Physical works of note:**

These exist as originals and represent finished, saleable work:
- Standing figure with striped skirt — frontal, iconic, Woodland school influence, holding wrapped bundle. Most compositionally complete physical piece.
- Spear figure — dramatic diagonal, purple-black cloak with orange circle motifs, sun and mountain.
- Tipi painting (acrylic) — four glowing tipis with pictographic marks, red buffalo silhouette, cross-hatched sun. His last analog painting; donated to auction benefiting preservation of a deceased Native woodcarver's tree carvings in Niwot, CO.
- Bear portrait — the most raw and confrontational piece in either catalog, physical or digital.
- Owl — direct physical ancestor of the bold outline digital style.

**Status:** Physical practice dormant due to lack of studio space, not lack of interest or skill. The hand is intact.

## Drawing practice: portrait studies and the Curtis archive

Tom draws portraits in pen — confident, expressive figurative studies that demonstrate solid understanding of facial structure, planes, and how to capture presence with minimal line. These are not warm-up sketches; they are research drawings.

**Source material: Edward Curtis**

The portrait sketches are made in direct response to photographs by Edward S. Curtis (1868–1952), whose project *The North American Indian* produced over 40,000 photographs of Indigenous peoples across more than 80 nations. Curtis is one of the most contested figures in the history of Indigenous representation:

- His photographs are technically extraordinary and document faces that exist nowhere else
- He staged many images — removing modern objects, dressing subjects in other nations' clothing, framing his project explicitly around the "Vanishing Indian" narrative
- His work shaped non-Indigenous perceptions of Native people for generations in ways that were often reductive and harmful

**What Tom is doing with Curtis:**

Drawing from Curtis as an Indigenous artist is a specific critical act — not passive reference. He is putting his hand between Curtis's lens and those faces. The drawing strips away the photographic staging, the colonial framing, and the vanishing race mythology to recover what is actually there: specific people with specific presence, age, and character. This is related to the practice of artists like Wendy Red Star (Crow/Apsáalooke), who explicitly engages with and recontextualizes historical photography of her people by centering the Indigenous subject rather than the photographer's intent.

The elder profile drawings especially — deeply lined faces, dignified bearing, long hair — carry this weight. Knowing they come from Curtis and knowing Tom is Haudenosaunee drawing them back through his own hand adds a decolonial dimension to what reads as straightforward figurative study.

**What the drawing practice contributes:**

- Foundational figurative skill underlying all the digital figurative work — when Deer Woman or MMIW have a real person present in them, it comes from here
- Deep familiarity with how Indigenous faces read at different ages, in different light, in different emotional states
- The sketch layer is where Tom learns a face before he interprets it digitally
- The Mohawk digital piece has a direct sketch ancestor in these studies

## Graphic, tattoo, and logo practice

Running parallel to the fine art practice is a significant body of graphic design, tattoo design, and illustration work. Same Indigenous visual vocabulary, different contexts and clients.

**Tattoo sleeve designs**

Multiple full arm/sleeve tattoo designs, each distinct and densely composed with Indigenous geometric and symbolic vocabulary: spirals, faces, triangles, hand symbols, dots, organic curves, geometric borders. These treat the human arm as a specific canvas with its own constraints — design must wrap a three-dimensional form and read at skin scale. This is specialized knowledge requiring deep familiarity with how Indigenous iconographic systems work applied to the body. Four or more distinct sleeve designs documented.

**Logo and identity work**

- *Indigenous filmmaker logo* — Indigenous woman at a film camera, clean black and white, minimal and iconic. Professional identity design.
- *Left Hand Series* — a mountain held in a decorated hand (sleeve with geometric marks). A named series concept, likely referencing Chief Niwot (Left Hand) of the Southern Arapaho, connecting to Boulder County / Colorado Indigenous history.
- *We Have Always Been Here* — statement piece combining text with petroglyph elements: medicine wheel, lizard/snake figure, spiral, sun. Functions as political statement, print, or merch design.

**Illustration and merch work**

- Outdoor/camping sticker sheet — RV in forest, UFO abduction scene, tipi camping, Bigfoot/Sasquatch, pine trees. Indigenous elements woven into outdoor culture humor. Merch territory; the overlap between outdoor culture buyers and Indigenous art buyers is real and underserved.
- Petroglyph-style brainstorming sheets — loose compositional studies with tipis, figures, birds, hand symbols, spirals. Working out iconographic arrangements.

**The howling wolf**

A single minimal piece: black wolf silhouette howling at a pink/rose circle, one teal line detail on the animal's body. The most graphically resolved piece in the entire graphic practice — exceptional economy of means. Could function as a logo, print, t-shirt, or tattoo. Unlike anything else in the catalog in terms of graphic restraint.

## Artistic instincts (observed May 2026)

- **Trusts restraint** — the most minimal pieces (MMIW, Warpaint) are among the strongest
- **Makes fast, clear decisions** — drops elements that aren't working without sentimentality
- **Knows when something needs a rebuild vs. a refinement** — doesn't over-preserve old work
- **The through-line holds across time** — instincts in early work and current work are consistent, execution has evolved
- **Land is always relational** — even landscape pieces imply presence, relationship, or stakes; never pure scenery
