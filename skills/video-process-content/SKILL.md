# video-process-content

Process videos are one of the most effective things an artist can share — they show work being made, which is more compelling to most audiences than a finished image alone. This covers how to capture, edit, and publish process content without it consuming all your time.

---

## What process video does well

- Shows the work coming alive — transformation from blank to finished
- Humanizes the artist — your hands, your decisions, your pace
- Explains what a still image can't — layering, iteration, revision
- Gets significantly more reach on Instagram and TikTok than static posts

What it doesn't do well: sell work directly. People watch and admire; they don't immediately click to buy. Process video builds the relationship that eventually leads to purchases.

## Capturing digital art process

### Procreate timelapse

Procreate records every stroke automatically. When you're done:

1. Go to **Canvas → Time-lapse Replay** to preview
2. Export: **Canvas → Time-lapse Video** → Full Quality (4K) or Standard Quality
3. Trim in your phone's video editor or a desktop app

The timelapse is fast by default. For Instagram Reels (15–90 seconds), the raw timelapse often works without additional editing if the piece took a few hours. For longer pieces, you may want to speed it up further or cut sections.

**Add music**: Procreate exports silent video. Add audio in editing.

### Screen recording on iPad

For a real-time capture with voiceover — good for tutorial-style content:

- iOS built-in screen recording captures everything on screen
- Include microphone audio in the recording (hold the screen record button and toggle mic on)
- Lower quality than the Procreate timelapse but captures your actual thinking in real time

### Desktop screen recording

For work in Photoshop, Procreate on Mac, or other desktop apps:

- **OBS Studio** (free) — full-featured, can record to file or stream live; records at any resolution and quality
- **QuickTime** (Mac) — simple, built-in; File → New Screen Recording
- **Screenflow** (Mac, paid) — combines recording and editing in one app

Recording settings: 1080p at 60fps is a good default. For timelapse from screen recording, record at normal speed and speed up in editing.

## Filming physical artwork

For work made with physical media, or for showing your workspace and hands:

### Camera options

- **Phone**: modern iPhones and Androids shoot excellent video. 4K if you have storage; 1080p at 60fps is very usable.
- **Mirrorless/DSLR**: better in low light, more control over depth of field, often better audio options. Worth it if you already have the camera.

### Setup for overhead (flat work)

An overhead rig captures hands working on paper, sketchbook, or flat canvas:

- Mount your phone overhead using a flexible arm clamp (Joby GorillaPod, desk mount arm)
- Light from the front and side — avoid shadows from your own hands
- Keep the camera parallel to the work surface to prevent distortion

### Lighting

Consistent, soft lighting is more important than bright lighting.

- Natural window light (overcast is ideal — no harsh shadows) works well if you can position near it
- Two softbox lights or ring lights on either side eliminate shadows
- Avoid mixing daylight and artificial light — the color clash is distracting

### Audio for physical process

The sounds of your medium — brush on paper, pencil, cutting — are satisfying and add to the video. Don't suppress them. Just make sure your environment is quiet enough that background noise doesn't compete.

## Voiceover

Voiceover adds a layer that silent timelapse doesn't have — your thinking, your decisions, your relationship to the work.

Two approaches:

**Narrate as you go** (real-time screen recording): captures spontaneous thought; sounds natural; requires you to talk while working.

**Record voiceover after** (record audio separately, lay under the video in editing): more polished; lets you plan what you'll say; easier to redo if you stumble.

Recording voiceover:
- Use a good USB microphone (Blue Yeti, Rode NT-USB, even the headphone mic from AirPods in a quiet room)
- Record in the quietest room available; close doors, turn off fans
- Keep sentences short and conversational — you're talking to someone, not reading a script
- Leave pauses — it's easier to cut silence than to add it

## Basic editing workflow

You don't need complex software. A simple workflow:

1. **Assemble** — put your clips in order on the timeline
2. **Speed up** — for timelapse content, increase playback speed to fit your target length
3. **Trim** — cut dead time at the start and end, and anywhere the video stalls
4. **Add audio** — music, voiceover, or both; balance the levels (music under voiceover should be quieter)
5. **Add text** (optional) — title card, captions, finishing text
6. **Export** — for each platform

### Tools

| Tool | Platform | Best for |
|------|---------|---------|
| CapCut | iOS/Android/Web | Fast mobile editing; great for Reels/TikTok |
| iMovie | iOS/Mac | Simple, free, sufficient for most needs |
| DaVinci Resolve | Mac/Win/Linux | Free, professional-grade; good if you want to learn properly |
| Premiere Pro | Mac/Win | Industry standard; subscription |
| Screenflow | Mac | Combined recording + editing for screen content |

For short Reels and TikToks: CapCut is fast and has features specifically built for vertical social video. For longer Patreon content: iMovie or DaVinci Resolve.

## Export settings by platform

| Platform | Orientation | Resolution | Length |
|----------|------------|-----------|--------|
| Instagram Reels | Vertical (9:16) | 1080×1920 | 15–90 sec (up to 15 min) |
| Instagram feed | Square (1:1) or 4:5 | 1080×1080 or 1080×1350 | Up to 60 sec |
| TikTok | Vertical (9:16) | 1080×1920 | 15 sec–10 min |
| YouTube | Horizontal (16:9) | 1920×1080 minimum | Any |
| Patreon | Any; horizontal common | 1920×1080 | Any |

For all platforms: H.264 codec, MP4 container, high quality. Most platforms transcode on their end — give them the best source you can.

## Patreon video content

Patreon supporters expect more than what you share publicly. Good Patreon video:

- **Longer and slower** — full real-time process, not condensed timelapse
- **More personal** — you're talking to people who've chosen to support you; be more candid
- **Exclusive** — content they can't see on Instagram or TikTok; early access, extended cuts, behind-the-scenes

Types that work well:
- Full process video (1–2 hours real-time, sped up to 20–40 minutes)
- Q&A / studio tour video
- "Why I made this" breakdown of a finished piece
- Works in progress that haven't been released yet

Don't over-produce Patreon content. Authenticity is the value; high polish can make it feel less personal.

## Music

Music changes the entire mood of a process video. Use it thoughtfully.

For public social posts: use royalty-free or licensed music. Using copyrighted music risks takedown or muting on Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube.

Good sources:
- **Instagram / TikTok audio library** (in-app): pre-licensed for use on platform; won't get you muted
- **Epidemic Sound** (subscription): royalty-free for social and YouTube
- **Artlist** (subscription): similar; good for YouTube
- **Free Music Archive**: free, various licenses — read each track's license before using
- **Your own recordings**: zero risk

For Patreon (behind paywall): rights are murkier. Royalty-free is still safest.
