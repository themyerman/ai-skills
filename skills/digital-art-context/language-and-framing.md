# Language and framing

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

---

## Medium terminology

These terms have specific meanings in digital art contexts. Using them precisely signals fluency; using them loosely invites confusion.

| Term | What it means |
|------|--------------|
| **Digital art** | Broad umbrella: any art made using digital tools or processes |
| **Digital painting / digital illustration** | Work made using software tools analogous to traditional painting or drawing (Photoshop, Procreate, Painter, Clip Studio Paint) |
| **Generative art** | Work produced by a system — code, algorithm, rule set — designed by the artist |
| **Algorithmic art** | Often used interchangeably with generative art; emphasizes the mathematical/rule-based nature of the process |
| **New media art** | Broader category: art using emerging technologies — video, internet, software, interactive systems — often with conceptual/critical intent |
| **Net art / web art** | Art made for and existing on the internet; distinct from art that is distributed online |
| **Software art** | Art in which software itself is the primary medium — the code is the work, not the output |
| **Glitch art** | Work that uses or aestheticizes digital errors, compression artifacts, data corruption |
| **Data visualization art** | Work derived from datasets, where the visual form encodes information |
| **Interactive art** | Work that changes in response to viewer input |
| **Immersive / experiential art** | Installation-scale work that surrounds the viewer; often uses projection or LED |
| **Computational art** | Historical term for early computer-generated work; sometimes used for contemporary algorithmic work |

## Print and edition terminology

For detailed print production guidance, see [visual-design / print-production](../visual-design/print-production.md). This table covers the market and framing vocabulary:

| Term | What it means |
|------|--------------|
| **Giclée** | High-quality inkjet print using archival pigment inks; the standard fine art output format for digital work |
| **Pigment print / archival print** | Synonyms for giclée, often used to avoid the term's dated associations |
| **Fine art print** | A print (any medium) produced for art market sale rather than commercial reproduction; implies archival materials |
| **Limited edition** | A set number of prints from a given work; after that number is reached, no more are produced |
| **Open edition** | Prints with no cap on quantity; lower price, no scarcity premium |
| **Edition of X** | "Edition of 25" means 25 prints exist from this work; numbered 1/25, 2/25, etc. |
| **Artist's proof (AP)** | Prints outside the numbered edition, retained by or gifted by the artist; typically ~10% of edition size |
| **Hors commerce (HC)** | "Outside commerce" — prints not for sale, used for exhibition or gifting |
| **Certificate of authenticity** | Signed document accompanying each print: title, date, edition number, total size, medium, and artist signature |
| **Provenance** | The ownership history of a work; critical for secondary market sales |
| **Variable edition** | Each print differs slightly (different color variant, etc.); common in generative art |

## Critical and curatorial vocabulary

Terms critics and curators use that come up in reviews, catalog essays, and panel discussions:

| Term | What it means | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| **Materiality** | The physical properties of a medium that influence the work | Critics often ask about digital work's "lack of materiality"; worth engaging rather than dismissing |
| **Indexicality** | The connection between an image and its subject (a photograph is an index of what was in front of the camera) | Digital images and AI images complicate indexicality |
| **Affordances** | What a medium or tool makes possible or easy | "The affordances of digital tools" = what the software enables |
| **Post-digital** | Work that treats digital as a given rather than a novelty; often mixes digital and analog processes | |
| **Remediation** | When one medium represents or absorbs another (digital painting remediating watercolor) | Bolter and Grusin; useful for discussing how digital tools relate to traditional media |
| **Dematerialization** | The shift from physical art objects to concepts, files, or ephemeral work | Has roots in 1960s conceptual art; applies differently to digital |
| **Aura** | Walter Benjamin's term for the unique presence of an original object; reproduced/digital work has no aura by his definition | The concept is contested; useful to know when it comes up |
| **Survivance** | Gerald Vizenor's term for active Indigenous presence and resistance against erasure | Relevant for Indigenous digital artists specifically — see [indigenous-art-americas / language-and-framing](../indigenous-art-americas/language-and-framing.md) |
| **Generativity** | The capacity of a system to produce varied outputs from a set of rules | Both a technical and a critical term in generative art discourse |
| **System aesthetics** | Jack Burnham's 1968 concept: art in which the system, not the object, is the artwork | Historical foundation for generative art criticism |
| **Process vs. product** | Whether the art is the making (process) or the result (product) | Digital work often foregrounds process — code, algorithm — as part of the art |

## Collector vocabulary

What collectors and galleries typically want to know about digital work:

- **Medium statement:** "Archival pigment print on [paper/canvas]" is the standard form. Include paper weight and brand for serious collectors ("Hahnemühle Photo Rag 308gsm").
- **Edition details:** Edition size, number within edition, any APs that exist. Collectors want to know scarcity.
- **Print dimensions:** Standard to list both image size and paper size if there's a border.
- **Year of creation:** The digital file's creation date, not the print date.
- **Software/process note:** Optional but sometimes asked for. "Created digitally using [tools]" is sufficient; no need to over-explain.
- **Framing recommendations:** Many collectors want guidance; archival framing with UV-protective glazing is standard advice for fine art prints.

## How to talk about digital work

### Describing your own process

Avoid being defensive or pre-emptively justifying the medium. Say what you did, not what you didn't do.

- Instead of: "Even though it's digital, it still takes real skill and time..."
- Say: "I work in Photoshop / Procreate / [tool], building up layers over several sessions."

### When asked "is it printed or original?"

The question usually means: is there a physical object, and is it unique? A reasonable answer for edition prints:

> "It's a limited-edition archival print — [edition size] exist, each signed and numbered. The file exists, but the prints are the market object."

### When asked about AI

If your work doesn't use AI generation: "This is hand-made in [tool] — no AI generation." Clear and specific.

If your work incorporates AI tools: be specific about what role AI played. "I used AI to generate initial compositions, then painted over and altered them substantially" describes a very different process than "I typed a prompt and exported the result."

### Framing pitfalls

**Don't apologize for the medium.** Every "but it's still real art" construction concedes the question before answering it.

**Don't over-explain the tools.** Collectors don't need to understand Photoshop to appreciate the work. Technical process is secondary to visual and conceptual content.

**Don't conflate digital art with AI art.** They're not the same thing. If the conflation comes up, correct it: "Digital tools and AI generation are different — I work in [tool], which is closer to a digital paintbrush than to an AI generator."

**Don't treat printmaking as lesser output.** For digital artists whose primary market is prints, the print *is* the artwork. A giclée edition by a serious artist is not a reproduction of a "real" artwork — it's the thing itself.
