## The legitimacy question

The foundational debate about digital art — "is it real art?" — has largely been settled in institutional terms. Major museums collect it; major auction houses sell it; major critics write about it. But the question persists in popular culture and in certain corners of the art world, and understanding its structure is useful.

The argument against digital art's legitimacy usually rests on one or more of:

1. **No unique object.** A digital file can be perfectly copied; there's nothing "original" in the traditional sense.
2. **No hand.** Software generates what a brush and skill used to produce.
3. **No irreversibility.** Undo removes the commitment and risk that characterize "real" art-making.
4. **Too easy.** Filters, tools, and automation reduce the required skill.

Each of these arguments has been made about photography (arguments 1, 2, 4), printmaking (argument 1), and even oil painting (when tube paint replaced hand-ground pigments, it was criticized as making painting "too easy"). The arguments are about legitimacy criteria, not about the work itself.

The useful counter-framing: medium determines neither quality nor significance. The decisions an artist makes — what to make, for whom, to what end — are not determined by what tools they use.

## Edition and scarcity

Because digital files are infinitely reproducible, digital artists working in the print market have adopted edition conventions borrowed from printmaking:

- **Limited edition:** a set number of prints produced from a given file (e.g., edition of 25). Once sold, no more are printed. The edition size is declared in advance and honored — the same trust structure as a screenprint edition.
- **Open edition:** prints sold without a cap; lower price, no scarcity. Common for commercial prints; less common for gallery-sold work.
- **Artist's proof (AP):** a small number of prints outside the numbered edition, retained by the artist or given to collaborators. Typically 10–15% of the edition size. Carries the same value as numbered prints.
- **Certificate of authenticity:** a signed document accompanying each print that records the edition number, total edition size, title, date, and medium. Not legally required but standard practice. Essential for resale value.

The edition structure is the mechanism through which digital art participates in the art market. It works because it's a social contract — buyer and artist agree to honor scarcity that is technically artificial.

## NFTs: what happened and what remains

### What NFTs were

An NFT (non-fungible token) is a unique entry on a blockchain that records ownership of a digital asset. The asset itself is usually not stored on the blockchain (files are too large); the token points to a file stored elsewhere. From 2020–2022, NFT platforms (OpenSea, Foundation, SuperRare, Nifty Gateway) enabled digital artists to sell work directly to collectors with provenance recorded on a public ledger.

### The boom and collapse

Beeple's *Everydays: The First 5000 Days* sold at Christie's in March 2021 for $69 million, marking the peak of mainstream attention. The NFT market exploded, then collapsed — valuations fell 90%+ from 2022 onward as speculative buyers exited and the broader crypto market contracted.

### What remained

- **New collector communities.** Collectors who entered the market during the NFT period continued buying digital art, including through more traditional channels.
- **Generative art platforms.** Art Blocks and fx(hash) (and later others) established legitimate markets for long-form generative art independent of NFT speculation.
- **Provenance infrastructure.** The ledger-based provenance model, whatever its speculative excess, solved a real problem: how do you prove ownership and authenticity of a digital file? That question remains relevant.
- **Artist revenue models.** NFTs enabled direct artist-to-collector sales and royalties on secondary sales — mechanisms that don't exist in traditional print markets. These ideas persist in various forms.

### The critical position

NFTs remain contested. Critics argue they enabled speculation masquerading as art patronage, generated enormous environmental cost (proof-of-work blockchains), and inflated valuations that damaged the market for serious digital art by association. Defenders argue they democratized access to the art market for digital artists who had previously been excluded from gallery systems, and that the infrastructure is genuinely useful independent of the speculative excess.

## Generative art

Generative art predates NFTs by decades (see [overview.md](overview.md)) but NFT platforms gave it a new audience and market. The defining characteristic: the work is produced by a system — code, algorithm, rule set — rather than entirely by direct human mark-making. The artist designs the system; the system produces the output.

Key distinctions:

- **The artist's role is system design, not output selection.** Generative artists write code that determines the range of possible outputs; they don't necessarily choose each resulting image.
- **Variation within a system** is a feature, not inconsistency. A generative series (like Tyler Hobbs' *Fidenza*) produces many unique outputs from a single algorithm; the visual coherence of the series reflects the system's design.
- **Chance is a tool.** Many generative works incorporate controlled randomness — parameters that vary within defined ranges. This is a formalized version of what painters do when they work wet-on-wet or drip.
- **Long-form generative art** (the Art Blocks model) typically produces a fixed number of algorithmically unique outputs from a single piece of code. The code is the artwork; the outputs are editions.

Generative art connects directly to the early computer art of Molnár, Mohr, and Cohen — the contemporary field is in some ways a rediscovery of this lineage.

## AI-generated art

AI image generation (Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Adobe Firefly, and others) is a distinct and specifically contested category. The debates are live, unresolved, and worth understanding clearly.

### The authorship question

AI image generators are prompted by text or image input and produce output statistically derived from training data. Who is the author — the person who wrote the prompt, the person who designed the model, the artists whose work was in the training data?

Current legal status: in the US, the Copyright Office has indicated that AI-generated images without "sufficient human authorship" are not copyrightable. What "sufficient" means is being litigated. Outputs with substantial human curation, selection, and iteration are treated differently than single-prompt outputs.

### The training data question

Most major AI image models were trained on large datasets of images scraped from the internet, often without the consent of the artists whose work was included. Class action lawsuits by artists against Stability AI, Midjourney, and others are ongoing. This is the most politically charged aspect of AI art — it implicates not just questions of authorship but of economic harm to working artists.

### The displacement question

Whether AI tools displace human illustrators, concept artists, and designers is empirically contested but directionally plausible in some markets. Illustrators have documented losing commercial commissions to clients using AI. The effect on fine art is less clear.

### The "is it art?" question (again)

AI image generation has revived the digital art legitimacy debate with new specifics. The arguments against: the artist didn't make the marks, the system plagiarizes from human artists, the prompt requires no skill. The arguments for: curation, iteration, and vision matter; the tool is new but the question of "how much is the artist doing?" applies to photography, printmaking, and digital art generally.

The honest position: AI-generated images exist on a spectrum from trivial (single unedited prompt output) to substantive (deeply iterative, heavily curated, integrated with other tools). The interesting question is not whether AI art "counts" but what decisions the artist made and to what end.

## Institutional reception

The art world's acceptance of digital art has been uneven and slow:

- **Major museums** (MoMA, Tate, Centre Pompidou, Whitney) have collected digital and new media work since the 1990s but inconsistently; digital works are underrepresented relative to their market share.
- **Preservation is a crisis.** Software becomes obsolete; hardware fails; operating systems change. A work made in Flash no longer runs. Rhizome's emulation-based preservation tools address this but the problem is structural.
- **Gallery systems** remain print-friendly and digital-cautious. Most commercial galleries still handle digital work as prints rather than files, which fits existing market infrastructure but limits what counts as "a digital artwork."
- **Auction houses** began handling digital work seriously after the Beeple sale but remain ambivalent; high-value digital sales are still news rather than routine.
- **Art fairs** have dedicated digital/new media sections (e.g., Art Basel's digital programming) but digital work remains a sideshow to painting and sculpture at most fairs.
