# Overview: digital art in context

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

## What "digital art" actually means

"Digital art" is a broad umbrella. It covers work that is:

- **Created digitally** — painting, drawing, illustration, collage, photo manipulation made with software
- **Generated algorithmically** — work produced by code, with or without human intervention at each step
- **Born digital and displayed digitally** — video art, interactive art, net art, immersive installation
- **Output as physical objects** — giclée prints, 3D-printed sculpture, laser-cut work

The term is least useful as a style descriptor and most useful as a process descriptor. A photorealistic digital painting and a generative abstract composition are both "digital art" in the same way that an oil portrait and a welded steel sculpture are both "studio art."

## A brief history

### 1960s–70s: Computer art and algorithmic beginnings

The first computational art predates personal computers. Artists working with mainframes and plotters explored what machines could generate:

- **Vera Molnár** (Hungarian-French) — one of the earliest systematic users of the computer to generate visual work; geometric, rule-based series from 1968 onward
- **Manfred Mohr** (German) — algorithmic works based on the structure of the hypercube; began 1969
- **Harold Cohen** — created AARON, an AI system that generated drawings; worked from 1973 through the 2010s
- **Charles Csuri** — early computer-generated animation and prints from the mid-1960s

This work was largely institutional (universities, Bell Labs) and received as curiosity rather than art world practice.

### 1970s–80s: Video art and digital photography

Video art (Nam June Paik, Bill Viola, Joan Jonas) established moving image as a legitimate fine art medium — a precedent directly relevant to later digital work. Digital photography began emerging in the 1980s, facing many of the same legitimacy questions digital art would face later: Is it "real" photography? Can it be trusted? Can it be art?

### 1990s: Net art and the first digital art market

The wide availability of personal computers and the emergence of the web produced net art — work that existed on the internet, often interactive, often ephemeral. Key figures: Olia Lialina, JODI (Joan Heemskerk and Dirk Paesmans). Institutions began paying attention: the Whitney Museum launched Artport (its online gallery for net art) in 2001; Rhizome (founded 1996) became a critical archive.

Simultaneously, digital tools (early Photoshop, Painter) entered artists' studios. The giclée print emerged as a fine art output format, enabling digital works to enter the print market.

### 2000s: Mainstream adoption and the museum question

Digital tools became ubiquitous in illustration, commercial art, and design — which complicated their status as fine art. The "is it art?" question intensified: digital work was everywhere in commercial contexts, which made its fine art aspirations harder to take seriously for some. Museums began collecting new media and digital work inconsistently; technical obsolescence became a preservation crisis.

### 2010s: Generative art goes mainstream, institutions catch up

Long-form generative art — algorithmic work where code determines composition — began reaching larger audiences. Tyler Hobbs, Vera Molnár (reassessed), and others built followings. Institutions including the Museum of Modern Art, Tate, and Whitney acquired significant digital and new media works. The conversation shifted from "can digital work be art?" to "how do we preserve and exhibit it?"

### 2021–present: NFTs, generative platforms, and AI art

The NFT market (2021–2022) created a brief, speculative explosion in digital art valuations — then collapsed. What remained: new collector communities, new platforms (Art Blocks, fx(hash)), and renewed interest in generative art as a distinct practice. Simultaneously, AI image generation (DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion) created a separate, contested category with live debates about authorship, training data, and displacement of human artists.

## Relationship to photography

Photography spent roughly 80 years arguing for recognition as fine art — from the Pictorialists of the 1880s through the establishment of photography departments at major museums in the 1970s. Digital art is having a compressed version of the same argument.

The specific points of overlap:

- **Reproducibility.** Both photography and digital art produce files rather than unique physical objects. Both fields developed edition conventions (numbered prints, certificates of authenticity) to create market scarcity from technically unlimited reproduction.
- **The hand question.** Photography was criticized for not involving the artist's "hand" — a camera does the work. Digital art faces the same criticism: the software does the work. In both cases, the criticism misrepresents how much intentional decision-making goes into the work.
- **Manipulation and trust.** Digital manipulation of photographs blurred photography's claim to documentary truthfulness. This is a photography problem, but it colored public perception of digital images generally.
- **Process concealment.** Both photographers and digital artists frequently conceal the technical process in favor of presenting the result. Both also have traditions that foreground process as part of the work.

## Relationship to printmaking

The connection between digital art and printmaking is closer and more specific than it first appears:

- **The giclée print** is a fine art inkjet print — pigment-based inks on archival paper or canvas — that emerged in the early 1990s as a high-quality output format for digital work. It enabled digital artists to participate in the print market on terms collectors already understood.
- **Edition structure** maps directly: digital prints are sold in numbered limited editions, with artist's proofs, exactly as screenprints or etchings are. The edition structure is a printmaking convention adopted wholesale.
- **The flat image on paper** as a format connects digital illustration and painting directly to the print tradition: the work is conceived for reproduction and exists fully in its printed form.
- **Historically, printmaking was "lesser."** Prints (engraving, etching, lithography) were long considered reproductive rather than original — tools for distributing compositions by "real" painters. This hierarchy collapsed in the 20th century. Digital art is navigating a version of the same status question.

## Relationship to painting

Digital painting tools (Photoshop, Procreate, Clip Studio Paint, Painter) allow artists to work in ways that are technically analogous to oil, watercolor, or gouache — pressure-sensitive brushes, layering, color mixing. The comparison invites both flattery and skepticism:

- **What's analogous:** mark-making decisions, compositional process, color relationships, the accumulation of layers over time
- **What's different:** infinite undo, non-destructive editing, the absence of physical resistance, no irreversible marks
- **The hierarchy question:** "Traditional" painters sometimes frame digital painting as easier or less skilled. This is usually wrong in the specific (digital mark-making has its own difficulties) and irrelevant in principle (medium difficulty is not the same as artistic quality). The argument mirrors earlier dismissals of photography.

The more interesting distinction is not difficulty but *materiality*: oil paint has physical properties that influence the work (drying time, viscosity, texture). Digital tools simulate but don't replicate that. Some artists experience this as freedom; some experience it as a kind of weightlessness that they actively work around.

## Key institutions and archives

- **Rhizome** (rhizome.org) — critical archive and new media art organization; maintains ArtBase (digital art archive) and Webrecorder (preservation tools)
- **Ars Electronica** (Linz, Austria) — annual festival and prize focused on art, technology, and society; Prix Ars Electronica is a major recognition for digital work
- **ZKM | Center for Art and Media** (Karlsruhe, Germany) — museum and research center focused on new media art
- **Whitney Museum Artport** — online platform for net art commissions
- **LACMA, MoMA, Tate** — all have made significant digital/new media acquisitions; MoMA's collection includes early computer art and net art
- **Art Blocks** — curated platform for generative art; established institutional credibility for long-form generative work
