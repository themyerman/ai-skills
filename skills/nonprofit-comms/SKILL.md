# nonprofit-comms

Nonprofit communications serve two masters: mission and money. Good comms make the mission visible and make donors feel like partners in it. This covers the practical writing and strategy decisions that come up running a small or mid-size nonprofit.

---

## The case for support

The case for support is the master document that answers why your organization deserves funding. Everything else — appeals, grant proposals, social posts — draws from it.

A strong case for support answers:
1. **The problem** — what's broken or missing in the world?
2. **The people affected** — who is experiencing this, and how?
3. **Your solution** — what does your organization do about it?
4. **Your evidence** — how do you know it works?
5. **The opportunity** — what could be achieved with more resources?
6. **The ask** — what do you need from donors?

Keep it to 2–4 pages. It should be readable by a board member, a donor, and a grant reviewer — all of whom have different levels of background. Use plain language.

Update it annually.

## Donor appeal letters

Appeal letters are direct solicitations. They work when they make the donor feel like the protagonist, not the organization.

Structure that works:
1. **Open with a story** — a specific person, place, or moment that illustrates the problem
2. **Pivot to impact** — how does your work change that story?
3. **Ask clearly** — specific dollar amount with what it does ("$50 provides materials for one student")
4. **Make it easy** — give them exactly one action to take
5. **Sign from a real person** — not "the team"

Rules:
- Write to one person, not "donors" — "you" and "your" throughout
- The words "you" and "your" should appear more than "we" and "our"
- One ask per letter — don't muddy it with multiple opportunities
- Keep it to one page if possible; two max
- P.S. is the most-read line after the first paragraph — use it to restate the ask

```
# Example P.S.
P.S. Your gift by [date] will be matched dollar-for-dollar by a generous
founding donor. Double your impact — give at [URL] today.
```

## Grant writing basics

Grant proposals are structured sales documents. They follow the funder's instructions exactly — read the RFP carefully before writing a word.

Core components most foundations ask for:

| Section | What they want |
|---------|----------------|
| Executive summary | Who you are, what you're asking for, what it will accomplish (1 paragraph) |
| Organization background | History, mission, credibility, key programs |
| Need statement | The problem — local data, national context, who's affected |
| Program description | What you'll do, how, for whom, timeline |
| Evaluation plan | How you'll know if it worked; what you'll measure |
| Budget | Line-item budget with narrative; show how grant funds are used |
| Sustainability | How the work continues after the grant ends |

Common mistakes:
- Writing about your organization's needs instead of the community's needs
- Jargon the funder won't recognize
- Vague outcomes ("increased awareness") instead of measurable ones
- Budget that doesn't match the narrative
- Missing attachments (IRS determination letter, financial statements, board list)

## Impact reporting

Impact reports document what your work accomplished — for donors, board, and funders.

What to include:
- Key numbers (people served, programs run, funds distributed)
- Stories behind the numbers (1–2 spotlight stories)
- Financial summary (revenue vs. expenses, program vs. overhead ratio)
- Quotes from participants or community members
- A thank-you to donors

Format options:
- Annual report (print or PDF) — comprehensive, annual
- Mid-year update email — shorter, conversational
- Grant report — funder-specific, tied to funded outcomes

Design principle: let the numbers breathe. A single stat in large type with a photo is more powerful than a table of 20 metrics.

## Stewardship

Stewardship is everything you do to thank, update, and retain donors after they give. It's often neglected and is one of the highest-ROI activities in fundraising.

Minimum stewardship by gift level:

| Gift level | What to do |
|------------|-----------|
| First gift at any amount | Thank within 48 hours; note it's their first gift |
| Any gift | Personalized thank-you letter within 2 weeks; tax receipt |
| $250+ | Personal call from ED or board member |
| $1,000+ | Quarterly personal update; invite to site visit or special event |
| Major gift | Individualized stewardship plan |

Never ask a donor for another gift before you've thanked them for the last one.

## Newsletter strategy

Nonprofit newsletters work best when they treat donors as insiders, not recipients of a press release.

Structure for a regular newsletter:
1. One main story — a person, a moment, a result
2. One update — program news, upcoming event, milestone
3. One ask or invitation — give, volunteer, share (not always a money ask)

Frequency: monthly is plenty for most small nonprofits. Bi-monthly or quarterly is fine if you can't sustain monthly quality. Don't sacrifice quality for frequency.

Subject lines that get opens:
- Specific over generic: "Maria finished her first year" beats "Update from [Org Name]"
- Curiosity or stakes: "What happened when we ran out of supplies"
- Personal: "A note from [Name]"

## Social media for nonprofits

Social media builds awareness and community — it rarely drives major donations directly. Treat it as relationship-building, not a revenue channel.

Content that works:
- Behind-the-scenes: what does a day look like for your team or participants?
- Milestone celebrations: a person's achievement, a program anniversary
- Community voices: quotes and stories from the people you serve (with permission)
- Calls to action: volunteer, event, petition, share — vary the ask
- Educational content: context about the issue you address

Ratio to aim for:
- 60% mission/story content (no ask)
- 20% community building (engagement questions, reposts)
- 20% calls to action (give, volunteer, event)

## Board communications

Board members need regular, clear information to govern effectively and advocate for the organization.

Monthly or meeting-cycle board packet should include:
- Financial summary (actuals vs. budget, cash on hand)
- Program updates (key metrics, highlights, challenges)
- Fundraising dashboard (pipeline, YTD vs. goal)
- Any decisions needed
- What's coming next

Keep it under 10 pages. Board members are volunteers with full-time jobs — dense packets don't get read.

One-pager format for board members to use when talking to donors:
- What we do (2 sentences)
- Who we serve (specific, concrete)
- What it costs / what their gift does
- How to connect them (website, contact)

## Telling your story with data

Data without story is forgettable. Story without data is unverifiable. You need both.

Pattern that works:
1. Lead with a specific person or moment
2. Zoom out to the number that shows this isn't an exception — it's the pattern
3. Come back to what the person's experience means now

```
# Example
Maria was the first in her family to apply to college.
She didn't know where to start.

This year, 47 students like Maria came to our program.
Thirty-two of them enrolled in higher education.

For Maria, that meant a scholarship to the state university —
and a path her parents didn't have.
```

Don't lead with the statistic. Lead with the human.
