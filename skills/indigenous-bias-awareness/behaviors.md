# Harmful behaviors and assumptions

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

---

## Harmful assumptions

These assumptions are widely held and frequently go unexamined. Most people who hold them have no overt prejudice — they absorbed them from decades of media, curriculum, and cultural shorthand.

### All Indigenous people are the same
Hundreds of distinct nations exist in North America alone, each with their own language, legal system, customs, spiritual traditions, and relationship to the land. Treating "Indigenous" as a single identity is the equivalent of treating all European nationalities as interchangeable.

### Indigenous people share a single language
There is no "Indian language." Indigenous languages are as distinct from one another as Japanese, Arabic, and Finnish. Many are endangered or already lost; many are being actively revitalized. Asking an Indigenous person to "say something in your language" assumes there is one.

### Indigenous spirituality is a monolith
Medicine wheels, dreamcatchers, talking circles, the sweat lodge, the vision quest — these are drawn from specific nations' traditions. Using them as interchangeable symbols of "Native spirituality" erases the specific meaning and context of each.

### Indigenous people were static before colonization
The pre-contact world was not a world of timeless, unchanging cultures. It was a world of empires, confederacies, trade networks, conflict, migration, and cultural evolution — often far more sophisticated than the "primitive" framing suggests.

### Indigenous cultures are past-tense
There are approximately 574 federally recognized tribal nations in the United States alone, with millions of citizens. Indigenous communities have contemporary governments, economies, schools, media, and artists. The assumption that Indigenous culture is historical — rather than living — is itself a form of harm.

### Any Indigenous person speaks for all Indigenous people
An Indigenous colleague, employee, or partner speaks for themselves and possibly their own nation. They don't carry the entire weight of speaking for every Indigenous person in every context.

### Indigenous people are defined by their appearance
Indigenous identity is not reliably visible. People of full, partial, or no visually apparent Indigenous heritage may hold tribal citizenship. Asking "You don't look Native — what are you, really?" is intrusive and delegitimizing.

### Living on a reservation means rural and poor; not living on a reservation means "not really Native"
Both framings are wrong. Reservation and urban Indigenous communities both exist, and neither is more or less authentically Indigenous. Most Indigenous people in the US live off-reservation.

## Harmful actions

### The "tomahawk chop" gesture
Associated with sports team culture. The gesture trivializes a tool with deep cultural significance to many Plains nations and combines it with invented "war chant" vocalizations — reducing complex cultures to props for fan entertainment.

### The "war whoop" or faux vocalization
A fabricated sound borrowed from 20th-century Hollywood Westerns. It has no specific root in any actual ceremonial or communicative practice. Using it (in sports contexts, as a joke, in imitation) is mockery.

### Wearing a costume headdress
A war bonnet (the feathered headdress most commonly represented in costume form) is earned through specific honorable deeds within specific Plains nations. It is not decorative, not generic "Native attire," and not a Halloween costume. Wearing one signals profound ignorance of its significance.

### Touching someone's regalia without permission
Regalia — the ceremonial clothing, adornment, and objects used in ceremony and at powwows — is personal, often has familial or spiritual significance, and is never to be touched without explicit invitation. This applies to all objects in ceremonial contexts.

### Taking photographs at ceremonies without permission
Many ceremonies are closed to non-members; others are open but not photographable. Assuming access and photographing without asking is a violation. When in doubt, ask before pointing a camera.

### Performing a ceremony or ritual element drawn from Indigenous traditions
Non-Indigenous people leading or participating in sweat lodges, smudging ceremonies, vision quests, or other ceremonial practices without explicit invitation from the relevant nation or tradition is appropriation. Some practices are sacred and closed. Some people have been seriously harmed by unqualified practitioners offering commercial ceremony experiences. For more on the appropriation vs. appreciation distinction, see [indigenous-art-americas / language-and-framing](../indigenous-art-americas/language-and-framing.md#4-decolonial-concepts-useful-for-conversation).

### Naming products or teams after nations or cultural elements without consultation
Winnebago, Apache, Mohawk, Jeep Cherokee, and countless other products use Indigenous names as brand assets without relationship or permission. In a professional context: naming a project, team, or product after an Indigenous nation, spiritual concept, or leader without meaningful consultation is appropriative.

### Praising "authentic" Indigenous aesthetics while devaluing contemporary Indigenous art
"I like traditional Native art, but contemporary stuff feels less authentic" applies a colonial filter: Indigenous people are most valued when they remain frozen in a pre-contact past. Contemporary Indigenous art is not less Indigenous for being contemporary. See [indigenous-art-americas / contemporary](../indigenous-art-americas/contemporary.md) for context on contemporary Indigenous art movements and artists.

## Harmful conversation topics

### Repeatedly asking an Indigenous person why a given term is offensive
One genuine question, asked sincerely, is fine. Requiring a detailed justification — or returning to relitigate the same question — places the burden of education entirely on the Indigenous person and signals doubt about whether their discomfort is legitimate.

### Demanding quantification of harm ("But it's just a word")
Harm from language is real regardless of intent. Asking an Indigenous colleague to prove mathematically that a term is harmful before taking action is not a good-faith request.

### Genocide denialism or minimization
"It wasn't really genocide." "That was a long time ago." "Both sides did bad things." These framings have been used to delegitimize Indigenous history and demands for justice. In a professional context they are deeply harmful and often constitute discriminatory behavior.

### Bad-faith debates about blood quantum and authenticity
Tribal citizenship is determined by tribal nations themselves, not by outsiders. Questioning whether someone is "enough" Indigenous based on appearance, behavior, or fractional heritage is not a legitimate discussion topic.

### Unsolicited opinions about reservation policy, sovereignty, or land rights
These are live political and legal matters with enormous stakes for Indigenous communities. Offering uninformed opinions in the direction of an Indigenous colleague ("But aren't reservations like apartheid?", "Shouldn't tribes just be absorbed into normal government?") treats serious self-determination questions as conversation topics.

### Asking an Indigenous person to explain their culture on demand
"Tell me about your traditions" or "What do your people believe?" puts an Indigenous person in the role of unpaid cultural educator. If you want to learn, do so from books, documentaries, and resources produced by Indigenous people — then bring specific questions if invited.

### Centering your own discomfort when correction happens
"I feel terrible now." "I didn't mean any harm." "You're making me feel attacked." When an Indigenous person corrects a harmful phrase or assumption, centering your own emotional response removes focus from the harm and places the Indigenous person in the position of managing your feelings.

## Quick reference: don't ask, don't do

| Don't ask | Don't do |
|-----------|----------|
| "What are you, really?" | Tomahawk chop or war whoop |
| "Can you say something in your language?" | Wear a costume headdress |
| "Do you live on a reservation?" | Touch regalia without permission |
| "Are you really Native?" | Take photos at ceremonies without asking |
| "What percentage are you?" | Lead or join closed ceremonies |
| "Can you speak for all Indigenous people?" | Use Indigenous names for teams or products without consultation |
