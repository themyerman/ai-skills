# Historical context

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

Understanding why anti-Indigenous bias is so normalized is different from cataloging what that bias looks like. This document covers the former: the structural history that made this bias so pervasive, so invisible to most people, and so persistent.

---

## The core logic

The simplest framework:

1. Colonizers want the land.
2. To take the land, they must either remove or justify the removal of the people who live there.
3. Justifying removal requires a framework that categorizes Indigenous people as less-than-fully-human, as obstacles, or as in need of "civilizing."
4. That framework, once built, gets encoded in language, law, media, and culture — and persists long after the original purpose is gone.

Every term in [terms-and-phrases.md](terms-and-phrases.md) and every behavior in [behaviors.md](behaviors.md) traces back to this logic.

## The tools of dispossession

### Trade and economic dependency
Initial contact between European powers and Indigenous nations was often framed as trade. The long-term effect was economic dependency: as nations adopted European trade goods (metal tools, firearms, cloth), traditional manufacturing knowledge eroded. Indigenous economic systems that had sustained communities for generations were replaced by dependency on European supply chains — which colonizers then used as leverage.

### Disease
The introduction of European diseases (smallpox, measles, influenza) killed somewhere between 50–90% of the Indigenous population of the Americas over roughly two centuries. This was not uniformly accidental: smallpox-infected blankets were documented as a deliberate weapon in at least one British military campaign. The demographic collapse made land acquisition radically easier and contributed to the view that Indigenous people were "vanishing" — a narrative that became self-serving for colonizers.

### Military force
When trade and disease were insufficient, military force was used directly. The US Army conducted campaigns against Indigenous nations throughout the 18th and 19th centuries. Massacres — Sand Creek (1864), Wounded Knee (1890), and many others — were often praised rather than condemned in contemporaneous press coverage.

### Treaties
The United States signed over 500 treaties with Indigenous nations. By 1871, Congress had passed legislation ending treaty-making; the existing treaties, most of which had already been violated, were left in uncertain legal status. Many remain the basis of ongoing legal disputes today. Treaties were routinely negotiated through coercion, fraudulent interpretation, or with leaders who lacked the authority to bind their nations.

### The reservation system
Reservations were created to concentrate Indigenous populations on small fractions of their original lands, often chosen because those lands were considered worthless at the time. When resources (oil, minerals, water) were later discovered on reservation land, that land was frequently taken again. The Dawes Act (1887) further subdivided reservation land into individual allotments, stripping another 90 million acres from tribal ownership over the following decades.

## The tools of erasure

After military defeat and land dispossession came a systematic effort to erase Indigenous identity itself.

### Residential and boarding schools
In the US and Canada, children were forcibly removed from their families and sent to boarding schools designed to eliminate Indigenous language and culture. The explicit mandate, articulated by Carlisle Indian School founder Richard Henry Pratt in 1892, was to "kill the Indian and save the man." Children were punished — sometimes violently — for speaking their own languages. The last US federal boarding school closed in the 1970s. The last Canadian residential school closed in 1996. Survivors and their descendants continue to deal with the intergenerational trauma of this system.

### Banning language and ceremony
The Indian Religious Crimes Code (1883) criminalized Indigenous ceremonial practices in the United States. Sun dances, potlatches, and other ceremonies were illegal. The ban on religious practice was not lifted until the American Indian Religious Freedom Act of 1978. This means that powwows — the ceremonies dismissed as synonyms for "quick meeting" — were illegal for nearly a century.

### Suppression of Indigenous languages
Removing children from their communities and punishing language use in residential schools was the most aggressive mechanism. The result: hundreds of Indigenous languages are now endangered or extinct. Language loss is not merely the loss of vocabulary — it is the loss of the conceptual frameworks, stories, and knowledge systems encoded in those languages. Language revitalization is one of the most urgent priorities across many Indigenous nations today.

### Blood quantum laws
The US government introduced blood quantum requirements — fractions of Indigenous ancestry — to define who qualified as "Indian" for federal purposes. The implicit effect over generations: the eligible population shrinks. Critics call this slow-motion termination. No other ethnic group in the United States is required to prove fractional heritage to claim identity.

## Why the bias became invisible

By the 20th century, the project of dispossession was largely complete in North America. What remained was its cultural residue — embedded in language, in Hollywood narrative conventions, in sports mascots, in children's play (cowboys and Indians), in curriculum that treated Indigenous history as complete rather than ongoing.

Several forces made this residue feel normal rather than harmful:

**Demographic marginalization.** Indigenous people make up approximately 1–2% of the US population. Concentrated in rural areas or urban Indigenous communities that are largely invisible to non-Indigenous media, they lacked the numbers to push back effectively against harmful representations.

**The "vanishing Indian" narrative.** The belief — propagated from the earliest colonial period — that Indigenous people were dying out "naturally" made their erasure seem like nature rather than policy. It also made the urgency of addressing anti-Indigenous bias seem academic: why address harm against a people who no longer exist?

**Romantic primitivism.** The flip side of the "savage" stereotype is the "noble savage" stereotype: Indigenous people as mystically connected to nature, spiritually pure, existing outside of time. Both the hostile and the romantic framings share a fundamental assumption — that Indigenous people are not fully modern humans with the full range of contemporary concerns, ambitions, and complexity. Both deny full humanity.

**Sports and media normalization.** The Washington NFL team's name, the Cleveland baseball team's mascot (both since retired), countless high school mascots, and decades of Hollywood Westerns normalized harmful representations to the point where objecting to them felt like oversensitivity rather than a reasonable response to harm.

## Why this matters professionally

Anti-Indigenous bias causes direct professional harm:

- Indigenous employees who hear harmful phrases must decide whether to correct them (and absorb the social cost of doing so) or ignore them (and absorb the cost of working in an environment that dismisses their experience).
- Harmful imagery and naming in products creates real reputational and legal risk, and real harm to Indigenous users.
- Organizations that ignore anti-Indigenous bias signal to Indigenous employees, partners, and communities that their experience is not valued.

Unlike some forms of bias, anti-Indigenous bias is frequently met with the response: "I had no idea that was offensive." That response is accurate — decades of normalization have made it genuinely invisible to most people who've absorbed it. The appropriate next step after "I had no idea" is not defensiveness; it's change.
