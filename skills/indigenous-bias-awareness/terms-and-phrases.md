# Problematic terms and phrases

**Hub:** [SKILL.md](SKILL.md)

**Note:** These terms are named explicitly so they can be identified. That's the point of this document.

**Important:** Just because Indigenous people may use some of these terms with each other does not give non-Indigenous people cover to use them — in a professional context or otherwise.

---

## Severity scale

- **Extremely High** — consider hate speech; no defensible use in any context
- **Very High** — deeply offensive; avoid entirely in professional settings
- **High** — offensive; causes real harm; clear alternatives exist
- **Medium/High** — context-dependent; avoid when in doubt

---

## US and Canada

### Redskin
**Severity:** Extremely High — consider hate speech

The most derogatory term used to describe Indigenous people in North America. Its prevalence as a professional football team name for decades normalized it among non-Indigenous people, but that normalization does not reduce the harm. The term's origin is tied to the bounty practice of paying for proof of Indigenous deaths.

**Say instead:** There is no reason to use this term. Refer to an Indigenous person by their name, nation membership, or title.

---

### Squaw
**Severity:** Extremely High — consider hate speech

A highly misogynistic term applied to Native women with a long history of derogatory use. Normalized through widespread use in place names across North America. The US Department of the Interior designated it derogatory in 2021 and began removing it from federal designations; many states are working with tribal nations to rename affected locations.

**Say instead:** There is no reason to use this term. Refer to Indigenous women by name. If referencing an unrenamed place, use "Sq**w" with a link to the relevant renaming process.

---

### Honest Indian / Honest Injun
**Severity:** Extremely High

A phrase meant to emphasize the truth of something — the racist equivalent of "I solemnly swear." Likely originated from settler assumptions that Indigenous people were duplicitous by default, unless proven otherwise.

**Say instead:** "I believe this to be true." "I'm being straight with you."

---

### Rez Dog
**Severity:** Extremely High in professional context

Slang for an Indigenous person who seems to be wandering through life without purpose — drawn from the image of feral dogs common on reservations. Popularized by the show *Reservation Dogs*. Completely inappropriate from non-Indigenous to Indigenous. Problematic even between Indigenous people in a professional context.

**Say instead:** There is no equivalent. Don't use it.

---

### Trail of Tears / Sand Creek / Wounded Knee (used casually)
**Severity:** Extremely High

These are specific historical events of forced removal, massacre, and genocide. Using them as metaphors for a difficult business situation ("this project was a trail of tears") trivializes ethnic cleansing and genocide. An executive who used "trail of tears" to describe a hard quarter later apologized — but never fully recovered their credibility with Indigenous colleagues.

**Say instead:** "This is a very bad situation." "We went through something really hard."

---

### He/she has gone off the reservation
**Severity:** Extremely High

Originally used to describe Indigenous people who left reservation boundaries — the historical remedy being forced return, often through violence. Now used to describe someone not following procedure, while carrying the full weight of that history.

**Say instead:** "He/she isn't following procedure." "They've gone their own way."

---

### He/she is on the warpath
**Severity:** Extremely High

Trivializes the deliberate, council-based process by which many nations made decisions about warfare — and reinforces the stereotype of Indigenous people as prone to violence and dangerous.

**Say instead:** "He/she is acting extreme." "They're very upset."

---

### Let's get together for a pow-wow
**Severity:** Extremely High in professional context

Powwows are detailed, ceremonially significant gatherings. Using the word as a synonym for "quick meeting" trivializes their importance and ignores that powwows and other public religious ceremonies were illegal in the United States until 1978.

**Say instead:** "We need to touch base." "Let's huddle." "Let's get together."

---

### My spirit animal is…
**Severity:** High

Extremely common in mainstream culture; trivializes specific tribal customs and ceremonies by which people come to understand their nature and place in the world. Often used as a synonym for "what I really identify with" or "my vibe."

**Say instead:** "My guiding principle is X." "My vibe is Y." "I really identify with Z."

---

### Hey, Chief…
**Severity:** Very High

Using "chief" as a casual salutation for an Indigenous person whose title isn't Chief trivializes Indigenous cultural protocols and "others" that person in front of colleagues.

**Say instead:** Use the person's name.

---

### Too many chiefs, not enough Indians
**Severity:** High

Reflects a Eurocentric view that Indigenous governance systems lacked strong central leadership — ignoring the actual sophistication and legitimacy of council-based decision-making across hundreds of nations.

**Say instead:** "We need more alignment." "We need a clearer direction." "We need someone to take the lead."

---

### Circle the wagons
**Severity:** Medium/High

The original context is the defensive formation of settler wagon trains against "Indian attacks." Now used to mean "get organized around a threat."

**Say instead:** "Let's get organized." "We need to close ranks on this."

---

### Hold down the fort
**Severity:** Medium/High

Derives from settlers building forts as protection against "Indian uprisings." Extremely common now, but the origin is what it is.

**Say instead:** "I'll be right back — take care of things while I'm away."

---

### Smoke the peace pipe
**Severity:** High

Among many Plains nations, the ceremonial pipe (often called a "peace pipe" by outsiders) was central to formal diplomatic, spiritual, and trade gatherings. Reduced to a metaphor for ending a disagreement.

**Say instead:** "Let's figure out how to end this disagreement."

---

### Low man on the totem pole
**Severity:** High

Totem poles encode clan history, family narrative, and significant events — they are not organizational hierarchies. In some cases the figure at the base is the most significant (foundational). The phrase trivializes a sacred object and misrepresents its meaning.

**Say instead:** "I'm not high in the hierarchy." "I find myself at the bottom of the pecking order."

---

### Indian time / Indian summer
**Severity:** High

Both phrases characterize Indigenous people as lazy, uncivilized, or indifferent to punctuality. "Indian summer" (late summer) and "Indian time" (being late) rely on the same derogatory stereotype.

**Say instead:** "He/she/they are late." For late summer: just say "late summer."

---

### Indian giver
**Severity:** High

Inverted a misunderstood aspect of Indigenous gift culture (where giving often involved ceremonial exchange and reciprocity) to mean someone who takes back a gift. Characterizes Indigenous people as duplicitous and transactional.

**Say instead:** "Let's have a fair exchange."

---

### Rain dance
**Severity:** Medium

Rain dances are highly ceremonial practices tied to harvest and survival. Used metaphorically to mean "wishing something would happen."

**Say instead:** "Let's hope this works out."

---

### Renegade Indian
**Severity:** High (complete phrase)

"Renegade" as a standalone word is neutral. Combined with "Indian" it carries specific history: Spanish colonizers used "renegado" for Indigenous people who resisted subjugation; later applied by US press to any Indigenous person who refused reservation life.

**Say instead:** "He/she isn't following our agreement."

---

### Papoose
**Severity:** High

An Algonquin word for baby that entered English primarily to differentiate Native babies from non-Native ones — as though their race made them a different kind of child.

**Say instead:** "Child." "Baby." "Toddler."

---

### Scalping
**Severity:** High

The practice of paying bounties for the scalps of Indigenous people is richly documented in colonial history, whatever the pre-contact origins of scalping may have been. Now used casually in financial contexts ("scalping tickets") or to mean "taking advantage."

**Say instead:** "I got taken advantage of." "I made a quick profit." (Depends on context.)

---

### Indian burn
**Severity:** High

The schoolyard bullying practice of twisting skin results in redness — a direct reference to "redskin." Unlikely in a professional context but worth knowing.

**Say instead:** "Twisted the skin on someone's arm."

---

### Bury the hatchet
**Severity:** High

Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) tradition holds that the founding of their Confederacy was sealed by burying a tomahawk. The phrase now means "make peace" in English.

**Say instead:** "Let's find an equitable agreement." "Let's put this behind us."

---

## Latin America

### Indio/India
**Severity:** Low to Very High depending on region — best to avoid

Dates to Columbus's first voyage, when he believed he had reached India. Common across Latin America but considered derogatory in many countries. Acceptability varies significantly by country and community.

**Say instead:** The specific nation name (Emberá, Kuna, Ngäbe-Buglé) or *indígena*.

---

### Cholo/Chola
**Severity:** High to Extremely High

In the US, an in-group term used by young urban Mexican Americans. In most of Latin America, a derogatory term for anyone with Indigenous ancestry. Etymological roots in a word originally meaning a mixed-breed dog of disreputable origin.

**Say instead:** The specific nation name or *indígena*.

---

### Salvaje
**Severity:** Extremely High

"Savage" in Spanish. Used to deride traditional Indigenous peoples through a civilized/savage binary that also connotes violence, sexual promiscuity, and lack of culture.

**Say instead:** The specific nation name or *indígena*.

---

### Primitivo/Primitiva
**Severity:** Very High

"Primitive" in Spanish. Same derogatory framework as *salvaje*.

**Say instead:** The specific nation name or *indígena*.

## Terms that are not offensive but have problematic associations

Some terms are not inherently offensive but frequently appear alongside harmful imagery or are commonly misapplied:

| Term | What's okay | What to watch for |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Pow-wow** | Referring to the actual event | Using as a synonym for any meeting |
| **Totem pole** | Northwest Coast carved cedar poles | Conflating with African or other traditions; misapplying to other contexts |
| **Peace pipe** | The ceremonial practice | Stereotypical depictions; trivializing use |
| **Tomahawk** | The actual tool | Sports "tomahawk chop" gesture; people in costume |
| **Eskimo** | Some people use it; many prefer Inuit, Yup'ik, Inupiaq | Can be a slur in some contexts; use the specific nation name when known |
| **Tipi, hogan, longhouse, wigwam** | Referring to actual dwellings | Stereotypical or mocking imagery attached to these terms |
| **Kachina** | Referring to Hopi spirit beings or the carved figures | Non-Hopi commercial reproductions are a persistent appropriation issue |
| Various nation names | Referring to nations accurately | Misapplied to products (Winnebago RVs, Apache helicopters, Mohawk haircut) |
